Act I · The question

The boardroom question

A campaign ran in one region. Sales rose. Marketing wants €4M to go national. You decide.

What you have:

  • Weekly sales for 30 metro markets, 60 weeks.
  • In week 40, exactly one metro switched on a €75k campaign for 20 weeks.
  • The other 29 did nothing. No randomisation: the metro was chosen.
  • Gross margin on incremental sales: 35% — hold that thought: it will make the bar iROAS > 1/margin ≈ 2.9, not iROAS > 1 (Act IV).

What that data forces you to answer, in order:

① How much did the campaign actually cause? attribution
② Is that more than luck? significance
③ Is the profit worth the price? the decision
The method this data calls for
  • One treated unit, observational data, an aggregate time series: the most common causal problem in marketing analytics, and no A/B test can be run after the fact.
  • Synthetic control is the field's standard answer. It is what Meta's GeoLift and Google's geo methodology run underneath.
  • The route: build the missing counterfactual (Act II), test it without a likelihood (Act III), turn it into euros (Act IV) and into a probability (Act V), then grade the whole machine against a real randomized experiment (Act VI).
"If the metro was not picked at random, isn't the test biased anyway?"
Split the worry in two:
  • Measuring what happened in that metro: no. The method never assumes the choice was random: it rebuilds the metro's own counterfactual from its own pre-launch record, so a choice based on pre-launch character (level, momentum, demographics) is absorbed.
  • Extrapolating to the other 29 markets: a separate assumption. One market's effect travels to the nation only if markets respond alike; and if the pilot was picked for promise, as is common in practice, its effect travels as a ceiling, not a promise: a NO-GO there is reinforced everywhere else, a GO must be discounted before it becomes €4M.
▸ The one poison the method cannot absorb
Timing the launch on private knowledge of a coming local boom: character-based selection is absorbed by the pre-period fit, but foreknowledge of a post-launch shock is not, because it leaves no fingerprint before launch. Act III has a test that hunts precisely that.

Act I · The question, and the world behind the data

The model that generated the data

This is a simulation, and that is a feature. You will never need to manipulate these equations; you do need to know what is inside them.
Why simulate at all
On real data the counterfactual is forever invisible, so a method can never be graded, only argued about. Here we planted the effect ourselves: every estimator gets marked against a known answer, and only then (Act VI) is it let loose on a real experiment.

Every market's no-campaign sales follow one equation: its own level \(\alpha_j\), plus its own exposures \(\gamma_j\) to three shared ingredients \(f_t\), plus noise. Expanding \(\gamma_j^{\top} f_t\) term by term gives the whole world in one line (all units €000):

\[ Y_{jt}(0) \;=\; \alpha_j \;+\; \gamma_j^{\top} f_t \;+\; \varepsilon_{jt} \;=\; \alpha_j \;+\; \gamma_{j1}\underbrace{\,0.4\,t\,}_{\text{trend}} \;+\; \gamma_{j2}\underbrace{\bigl[\,8\sin(2\pi t/26)+4\sin(2\pi t/13)\,\bigr]}_{\text{seasonality}} \;+\; \gamma_{j3}\underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{s=1}^{t}\eta_s}_{\text{macro walk }m_t} \;+\; \varepsilon_{jt} \]

Two ingredients matter most: the macro walk \(m_t=\sum_{s\le t}\eta_s\), a running sum of shocks that wanders like an economy (\(\mathrm{Var}(m_t)=t\,\sigma_\eta^2\), growing with time), and the exposures \(\gamma_j\sim\mathrm{U}(1{-}s,1{+}s)\): each market responds to the shared world its own amount. The spread \(s\) is what kills parallel trends.

▸ All five ingredients, one by one
  • Trend \(0.4\,t\): steady growth of 0.4 (€000) per week. Deterministic.
  • Seasonality \(8\sin(2\pi t/26)+4\sin(2\pi t/13)\): a 26-week half-year cycle of amplitude 8 and a 13-week quarterly cycle of amplitude 4. Identical for every market.
  • The macro walk \(m_t\): one fresh shock \(\eta_s\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_\eta^2)\) per week, none ever forgotten. This is "the economy".
  • Exposures \(\gamma_j\): each market multiplies each ingredient by its own loading.
  • Noise \(\varepsilon_{jt}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_\varepsilon^2)\): the market's own weekly wobble, \(\sigma_\varepsilon\approx 3\).
Which of these is ever observed?
Only \(Y_{jt}\) is written to the file. The level \(\alpha_j\), the loadings \(\gamma_j\), the factors \(f_t\), the noise \(\varepsilon_{jt}\): all of them exist in the simulator's code, and their real-world counterparts (market size, sensitivity to the season, the state of the economy) exist in reality, but none of them appears in any dataset you will ever load. Every estimator in Act II is a different way of coping with that.

On top of that no-campaign world we plant the effect:

\[ \tau_t \;=\; 0.12\,\bigl(\alpha_1+\gamma_1^{\top}f_t\bigr)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{t\ge T_0\}} \]

A 12% lift on the treated metro's no-campaign baseline, switched on at launch week \(T_0=40\): the indicator \(\mathbf{1}_{\{t\ge T_0\}}\) is 1 from launch onwards and 0 before, so the campaign adds nothing to the pre-period.

In the units the business uses, the planted truth is ·/week on average, · over the 20 campaign weeks. From here on we pretend not to know it, and grade every procedure by how much of it it recovers.

Act II · The counterfactual problem

Causal inference is a missing-data problem

Write the treated metro's sales in week \(t\) two ways: \(Y_t(1)\) with the campaign, \(Y_t(0)\) without. (Only the treated metro is in play here, so the market index is dropped.) The causal effect is the difference between two worlds:

\[ \tau_t \;=\; Y_t(1)-Y_t(0), \qquad \tau \;=\; \sum_{t\ge T_0}\tau_t \]

\(\tau_t\) is the effect in week \(t\); the sum \(\tau\), the 20-week total, is the estimand the business actually buys. After launch we observe \(Y_t(1)\). We never observe \(Y_t(0)\): the fundamental problem of causal inference. Every method in this course is a different way to fill that cell.

Act II · The counterfactual problem

Abadie's idea: if no twin exists, build one

The missing cell is one market's sales in a world where it never advertised. No single donor is that market's twin: each is too big, too small, or too differently exposed. So stop shopping for a twin and manufacture one.

Assume untreated sales follow a factor structure: the equation you already know from slide 4, now taken as the assumption rather than the recipe.

\[ Y_{jt}(0) \;=\; \alpha_j \;+\; \gamma_j^{\top} f_t \;+\; \varepsilon_{jt} \]
  • \(f_t\): the latent common factors. Their week-to-week movements are the "shocks": the macro wave rising, the season turning. Every market feels them, but not equally.
  • \(\gamma_j\): market \(j\)'s loadings, how strongly it responds to each factor. A tourist town has a big season loading; a commuter suburb a big macro loading. \(\alpha_j\) is its own level. In real data none of these are observed.
  • In the simulation \(f_t\) is literally trend + season + macro walk. On real data you never learn what the factors are; the method works without naming them.

Now take a weighted blend of donors. Its level and loadings are the blend of theirs. If the weights make them equal the treated metro's:

\[ \sum_j w_j\,(\alpha_j,\;\gamma_j) = (\alpha_1,\;\gamma_1) \;\Longrightarrow\; \sum_j w_j\,Y_{jt} \approx Y_{1t}(0)\ \ \text{for all } t \]
  • Same loadings \(\Rightarrow\) same response to every factor movement, including movements that have not happened yet. That forward guarantee is the whole trick.
The key intuition
DiD matches a level. Synthetic control matches the response to shocks. That is why it survives the non-parallel trends that break DiD. The next slide shows this happening.
Pedigree, and the papers' notation
Abadie & Gardeazabal (2003); Abadie, Diamond & Hainmueller (2010): "arguably the most important innovation in the policy-evaluation literature in the last 15 years" (Athey & Imbens 2017). The literature writes the factors \(\lambda_t\) and the loadings \(\mu_j\), and adds a common shock \(\delta_t\) felt identically by every market; any cross-market comparison cancels \(\delta_t\), so nothing changes here. The same machinery now prices ad campaigns.

Act II · The counterfactual problem

The estimator, precisely

Fit the weights on the pre-period only, constrained to the simplex:

\[ \hat w \;=\; \arg\min_{w\in\Delta}\; \sum_{t \lt T_0}\Bigl(Y_{1t}-\sum_j w_j Y_{jt}\Bigr)^{2}, \qquad \Delta=\Bigl\{w : w_j\ge 0,\ \textstyle\sum_j w_j=1\Bigr\} \]
  • \(\sum_{t\lt T_0}(\cdot)^2\): plain least squares on the 40 pre-launch weeks. The post-period is never shown to the optimiser, so the fit cannot "learn" the effect it is about to measure.
  • \(\Delta\) is the simplex: weights are non-negative and sum to one. The synthetic is a weighted average of donors, an interpolation, never "−80% of Milan + 190% of Rome".
  • No likelihood, no prior, no standard error. This is constrained least squares (solved by SLSQP), and it ships a point estimate only. Inference comes later, from placebos.
If you know regression, you already know this
Ordinary regression predicts an outcome from variables, one row per observation. Flip the table on its side: here the donor markets are the variables and the 40 pre-launch weeks are the observations. Synthetic control is a regression of the treated metro's pre-period on the donors' pre-periods, with the coefficients forced to be portfolio weights. Everything special about the method lives in that constraint.

Then read the effect off the post-period gap:

\[ \hat\tau_t \;=\; Y_{1t}-\sum_j \hat w_j Y_{jt}, \qquad\qquad \hat\tau \;=\; \sum_{t\ge T_0}\hat\tau_t \] \(\hat\tau_t\): treated minus synthetic in week \(t\)  ·  \(\hat\tau\): the 20-week total the business buys
Why constrain at all?
The simplex buys three things: interpretability ("our metro behaves like 40% dma_08 + 32% dma_20 + 17% dma_03" is a sentence a planner can act on), regularisation (most weights land on exactly zero), and an off-switch (a market no blend can match fails loudly instead of extrapolating). The next slide shows the evidence.

The interval is the real disagreement
A forecaster's prediction interval prices forecast error under the training distribution: "how wrong am I in weeks that look like the weeks I saw?" The question the campaign asks is different: "is my reconstruction of a world that never happened right?" Cross-validation, held-out weeks, backtesting — all of them score the first question. Its weights are an inspectable claim (slide 9's loadings; the n_eff you will meet on the model slide); a boosted tree's counterfactual is a claim you cannot audit.
  • The forecaster is better at prediction. The simplex is better at the job.

Every "obvious" estimator is a claim about \(Y(0)\)

You have built one estimator. Here are the three shortcuts a boardroom would have reached for instead, each run on the same simulated panel, each graded against the planted truth of €14.2k/week.
\[ \hat\tau^{\text{BA}} \;=\; \bar Y_{1,\text{post}} - \bar Y_{1,\text{pre}} \]

Before/after. Implied \(Y(0)\): "sales would have stayed at their pre-launch average." Assumes no trend, no season, no macro wave. On the case dataset: · vs truth €14.2k/wk.

\[ \hat\tau^{\text{T-C}} \;=\; \bar Y_{1,\text{post}} - \bar Y_{\text{ctrl},\text{post}} \]

Treated vs average control. Implied \(Y(0)\): "our metro sells what the average metro sells." Assumes one shared level, false the moment markets differ in size. Case dataset: ·.

\[ \hat\tau^{\text{DiD}} \;=\; \Delta_{\text{treated}} - \Delta_{\text{ctrl}}, \quad \Delta = \bar Y_{\text{post}} - \bar Y_{\text{pre}} \]

Difference-in-differences. Implied \(Y(0)\): "our metro moves in lockstep with the average control." Removes the level gap but assumes parallel trends. Case dataset: ·.

\[ \hat\tau^{\text{SC}} \;=\; \bar Y_{1,\text{post}} - \textstyle\sum_j w_j\, \bar Y_{j,\text{post}} \]

Synthetic control: the one you just fitted. Implied \(Y(0)\): "a weighted blend of donors that matched us pre-launch keeps matching us." Assumes matched exposures, and earns that assumption by fitting. Case dataset: ·.

The problem on a graph — and the repair, one toggle away

Notice what this graph does not contain: an arrow into \(X\). The factors \(f_t\) feed every market's sales, each through its own loadings \(\gamma_j\), and the treated metro's \(\gamma_1\) is nobody's average. Whatever fixed control you subtract, the comparison keeps a mismatch:

\[ \hat\tau^{\text{T-C}} \;=\; \bar\tau \;+\; \underbrace{(\alpha_1-\bar\alpha)}_{\text{level gap}} \;+\; \underbrace{(\gamma_1-\bar\gamma)^{\top}\bar f_{\text{post}}}_{\text{factor mismatch}} \;+\; \text{noise} \]
  • In words: besides the truth \(\bar\tau\) (the average weekly effect; slide 7's total is \(\tau=20\,\bar\tau\)), the estimate keeps the size gap, and the difference in how the two respond to the world times whatever the world did after launch. DiD removes the level gap and nothing else — and with one treated market, nothing averages the mismatch away.
  • The repair (tick the box): a donor blend built to carry the same loadings, \(\sum_j w_j\gamma_j \approx \gamma_1\), rides the same latent \(f_t\); the gap \(Y_{1t}-\sum_j w_j Y_{jt}\) cancels the factors. The mismatch dies at any spread \(s\), where DiD had to hope for \(s\to 0\). The pre-period fit you achieved on the mixer is the evidence the footprints match.
▸ Why not just control for the confounders?
Controlling means conditioning on recorded variables, and the variables doing the damage were never recorded: no column of the file contains \(f_t\) or \(\gamma_1\). You cannot adjust for what was never written down. What rescues us is structure, not measurement: the factors are shared and the loadings stable, so the donors' observed sales are live readings of the same unmeasured \(f_t\).
▸ And if the metro was picked for its character?
Plausible in practice: firms test where the outlook is good. The graph then gains one arrow, \(X \leftarrow f_t\): a textbook back-door — the same \(f_t\) drives both the choice to advertise and the sales. One cure, both readings: the same loading-matching closes the back-door too. Synthetic control never needs to know which world it is in.

Push the world to its limits, on paper

Substitute the factor model into DiD (treated change minus control-average change, post minus pre) and the estimator decomposes exactly. Then send each dial of the simulator to zero and read off what dies.
\[ \hat\tau^{\text{DiD}} \;=\; \bar\tau \;+\; \underbrace{(\gamma_1-\bar\gamma_C)^{\top}\,\Delta\bar f}_{\text{systematic bias }B} \;+\; \underbrace{\Delta\bar\varepsilon_1-\Delta\bar\varepsilon_C}_{\text{idiosyncratic noise}} \]
  • \(\Delta\bar f\): the post-minus-pre drift of the three factors (trend, seasonality, macro walk). \(\bar\gamma_C\): the \(n\) controls' average loadings. \(\Delta\bar\varepsilon\): the same post-minus-pre change in the idiosyncratic noise.
  • \(B\) is mean-zero over the exposure draw (every loading averages to 1), but your world is one draw: in it, \(B\) is a fixed nonzero number whose typical size is
\[ \operatorname{Var}(B) \;=\; \frac{s^{2}}{3}\Bigl(1+\frac{1}{n}\Bigr)\Bigl[(\Delta\bar f_{\text{trend}})^{2}+(\Delta\bar f_{\text{season}})^{2}+(\Delta\bar f_{\text{walk}})^{2}\Bigr] \]
▸ Where the \(\tfrac{s^2}{3}\bigl(1+\tfrac1n\bigr)\) prefactor comes from
Each loading is drawn from \(\mathrm{U}(1{-}s,\,1{+}s)\), a uniform of width \(2s\), whose variance is \((2s)^2/12 = s^2/3\). The treated metro contributes \(\operatorname{Var}(\gamma_1)=s^2/3\); the average of \(n\) independent controls contributes \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar\gamma_C)=s^2/(3n)\). Independent draws add, so \(\operatorname{Var}(\gamma_1-\bar\gamma_C)=\tfrac{s^2}{3}\bigl(1+\tfrac1n\bigr)\) per factor. Each factor's mismatch is then scaled by that factor's post-minus-pre drift, and the three squared drifts sum: the bracket.
Limit 1 · loading spread \(s \to 0\): sufficient on its own
  • The prefactor \(s^2/3\) kills the whole bracket at once.
  • Every loading \(\to 1\), so \(\gamma_1-\bar\gamma_C\to 0\) deterministically: exact parallel trends, macro walk included.
  • DiD then recovers \(\bar\tau\) up to noise.
Limit 2 · macro shock \(\sigma_\eta \to 0\): not sufficient
  • Only the walk's term \((\Delta\bar f_{\text{walk}})^2\) drops out.
  • Trend and seasonality still drift between the windows, and markets still weight them differently whenever \(s>0\): \(B \neq 0\).
  • What it buys: it removes the one stochastic, horizon-growing confounder; the bias that remains is at least deterministic.
  • Notice what is absent from \(B\): the amount of data. \(\Delta\bar f\) is the world's drift, not sampling error; more weeks shrink only the noise term, and with one treated market nothing ever averages over its own loading draw. Data buys precision around the same biased number.
  • This is why you fitted a blend instead of averaging the donors. DiD is stuck with the equal-weight \(\bar\gamma_C\) and must hope \(s\) is small. Synthetic control replaces it with fitted weights \(\sum_j w_j\gamma_j\) built to match \(\gamma_1\), which kills \(B\) at any spread — the repair you just toggled on the graph.
▸ The noise term, and the honest claim
The idiosyncratic noise survives both limits: it carries \(\sigma_\varepsilon \approx 3\) whatever \(s\) and \(\sigma_\eta\) do. The honest claim in a limit is "the systematic bias vanishes", never "DiD equals the truth".

Act III · Is it real, and how big?

€260k. Real, or a lucky metro?

Before answering, remember how "is it real?" is normally tested, and why none of that machinery works here.

With an A/B test you have many treated units. The recipe is a century old:

\[ t \;=\; \frac{\bar Y_{\text{treated}} - \bar Y_{\text{control}}}{\mathrm{SE}}, \qquad \mathrm{SE} \;=\; \sqrt{\tfrac{s_T^2}{n_T} + \tfrac{s_C^2}{n_C}} \] the standard error is estimated from the spread across treated units, and the CLT turns \(t\) into a p-value

Every piece needs replication: \(s_T^2\) is a variance across treated units, and the normal approximation needs \(n_T\) to grow.

Here:

  • \(n_T = 1\). A variance across one unit does not exist; there is no SE to put in the denominator.
  • No likelihood was written down anywhere, so there is no model-based standard error to fall back on either.
  • Asymptotics in \(n_T\) have nothing to bite on. The t-test is not weak here; it is undefined.
The way out
Change what you randomise over. We cannot replicate the treated metro, but we can ask how big a gap the method finds in markets where nothing happened. That comparison set is buildable from the data we already have.

From a test to an interval: inversion

You have one number, €260k, and one question: which true lifts could have produced it? Ask the placebo test about every candidate, and the survivors form the interval. No bell curve is used anywhere.
  • What the placebos already gave you: the estimator's error. Run on a market with a true lift of zero, the method still reports something: the cloud, centred on 0, spread ±€50k. That spread is this estimator's mistake distribution.
  • The one assumption. That the mistake does not change size when the truth is not zero. So if the true lift were \(H\), the estimator would report \(H\) + the same cloud: the cloud slides right, its shape untouched. That is the blue cloud in the figure, and the slider moves it.
  • Test one \(H\). \(H\) survives if our actual €260k lands inside the middle 90% of its slid cloud. If €260k falls in the tails, that \(H\) would have made our data a freak event: reject it.
\[ \underbrace{H + q_{0.05} \;\le\; 260 \;\le\; H + q_{0.95}}_{\text{\(H\) survives: our gap is an ordinary draw under \(H\)}} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \underbrace{260 - q_{0.95} \;\le\; H \;\le\; 260 - q_{0.05}}_{\text{the same statement, solved for \(H\)}} \]
  • That rearrangement is the whole trick, and it is where the name comes from. The left side asks "is the data ordinary under this \(H\)?"; solving the inequality for \(H\) turns a test you run once per hypothesis into a range of \(H\) you can read off directly. Inverting the test is the interval.
  • With the placebo quantiles \(q_{0.05}=-75\) and \(q_{0.95}=+65\): \(\;[260-65,\ 260+75] = \) [€195k, €335k]. Note the asymmetry is inherited from the placebos, not imposed.

Act V · Adding a probability, starting with a ten-minute Bayes primer

Bayes: beliefs, updated by data, as distributions

\[ \underbrace{p(\theta \mid \text{data})}_{\text{posterior}} \;\propto\; \underbrace{p(\text{data} \mid \theta)}_{\text{likelihood}} \;\times\; \underbrace{p(\theta)}_{\text{prior}} \]

\(\theta\) is whatever you do not know. In this warm-up \(\theta\) is the campaign's weekly lift in €k, and the data are the 20 weekly gaps your synthetic control just produced (their mean is €13.0k/week, the shipped €260k spread over 20 weeks). The posterior is a full distribution over \(\theta\), not a point and not a yes/no.

  • Prior \(p(\theta)\): what you believed before the data (grey, dashed).
  • Likelihood \(p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\): how well each candidate \(\theta\) explains the 20 observed weeks (orange).
  • Posterior \(p(\theta\mid\text{data})\): the compromise, weighted by precision (blue). A distribution can be integrated: \(\mathbb{P}(\text{profit} \gt c)\), expected loss, value of information. A point estimate and a p-value can do none of that.
▸ Two caveats, so the toy does not mislead
The real model's estimand is the 20-week total \(\tau\), not the weekly rate; and this warm-up treats the 20 gaps as independent, which the endnote will show is the very assumption that breaks. Read it as calibration for your eye, not as the shipped model.

Against the default sd-8 prior the data carry · of the posterior's weight: slide the prior mean and the blue curve barely moves. The same synthetic control, as a probability model

\[ w \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\mathbf{1}_{29}), \qquad \sigma \sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(5) \] \[ Y_{1t} \mid w,\sigma \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\Bigl(\sum_j w_j Y_{jt},\ \sigma^{2}\Bigr), \qquad t \lt T_0 \]

How the posterior of \(\tau\) is built. Each posterior draw of \(w\) is a full counterfactual path, so each draw yields one 20-week total. The estimate arrives as a distribution:

\[ \tau^{(s)} = \sum_{t\ge T_0}\Bigl(Y_{1t} - \sum_j w_j^{(s)} Y_{jt} - \varepsilon_t^{(s)}\Bigr), \quad s=1,\dots,S \]
  • Each draw carries both uncertainties: which blend is right (the weights) and the market's own weekly noise \(\varepsilon_t^{(s)}\). These are posterior-predictive draws.

Act VI · On real data, a randomized geo experiment

The real thing: a Google geo experiment

Google's public geo-testing dataset: a real ad campaign, run as a genuine randomized experiment across 100 US geographic markets.
  • 100 geos, randomized in matched pairs: 50 treated, 50 control.
  • 93 days of daily sales per geo: 42 pre-launch days, a 28-day campaign (day 42 onward), then 23 cooldown days.
  • Ad spend: $50k, all of it inside the campaign window. Pre-period spend is exactly zero.
  • Baseline: about $ of sales per geo per day before launch. During the campaign the treated arm runs +$ per geo per day above the concurrent control: ~% of the no-campaign level.
  • Against its own pre-period the same lift reads ~% — Act II's rejected denominator: the shared wave lifted every geo during the window, and the concurrent control is the honest baseline.
Why this dataset, specifically
  • It is randomized, so treated-minus-control is unbiased by design: a referee that depends on no model.
  • What the coin does and does not do: it cuts any assignment arrow (\(f_t \to X\) is gone), but the shared factors still drive every geo's sales.
  • With 50 treated geos that surviving influence averages out across the group, which is what makes the unbiased referee also precise enough to referee; one treated geo would have left Act II's factor-mismatch term standing.
  • So: pretend the randomization did not exist, run synthetic control as if this were our one-metro problem, and grade it against the referee.

The most important output: "I can't"

Try to build a synthetic for the country's largest market and the pre-fit gate fires:

\[ \text{pre-RMSE} = \$868 \;\;\gg\;\; \text{gate} = \$665 \]
  • Where $665 comes from: the same rule as the simulation's gate — pre-RMSE must beat a third of the lift the fit itself claims. The 50-geo cell claimed ~${{nb07b.post_gap}}/geo/day, so its bar was ${{nb07b.gate_ref}} (and it passed); this geo's fit claims a daily lift of nearly $2,000 (3 × $665), and still cannot track the pre-period to a third of that.
  • Why it fires: simplex weights can only average. No convex combination of smaller metros can reach the largest one, which sits outside its donors' hull.

Had the gate not fired, the shipped estimate would have implied an iROAS of 5.1, where the randomized experiment says 2.94. The gate, not hindsight, is what keeps that number out of the memo.

Why this is the honest headline
A method that always returns a number is more dangerous than one that sometimes says "this market is not reconstructable: get more comparable donors, or run a real experiment." The convex-hull constraint is what lets synthetic control know when it is out of its depth.

The off-switch is the feature, not the failure. Trust the tool that tells you when to stop.

Act VI · The verdict

What transfers beyond this case

1 · The estimator is the identification strategy
Before/after, DiD, synthetic control differ only in what they assume about the missing \(Y(0)\). Choose the assumption you can defend.
2 · Match responses to shocks, not levels
Simplex weights fit the treated unit's factor exposures; the twin survives the non-parallel trends that break DiD.
3 · With one treated unit, rank, don't t-test
Placebo permutation gives exact finite-sample inference; inverting it gives the interval. Power = donor count: the floor is \(1/(J{+}1)\).
4 · Profit = margin × lift − cost
The break-even iROAS is 1/margin, not 1. Significance and worthiness are different questions.
5 · Classical decides; Bayes prices
The design-based interval reached the action alone. The posterior added the probability, the headroom price, and the value of information: the board's numbers.
6 · Decide on probabilities, not means
A right-skewed posterior can put the mean above cost while most of the mass sits below it. \(\mathbb{P}(\text{profit}\gt c)\) is the number to sign.

Advanced note

Why the sum breaks, and the one-parameter repair

Diagnosis. The blend never matches the loadings exactly; the unmatched slice of the macro random walk \(m_t\) sits in the gap as a persistent offset \(\omega\): one draw per world, held for the window. The default likelihood assumes independent weekly errors, which prices \(\sigma_\omega\) at zero:

\[ e_t = \omega + \varepsilon_t \;\Rightarrow\; \mathrm{Var}\Bigl(\sum_{t=1}^{n} e_t\Bigr) = n^{2}\sigma_\omega^{2} + n\,\sigma_\varepsilon^{2} \]
  • vs the iid claim \(n(\sigma_\omega^{2}+\sigma_\varepsilon^{2})\): the offset compounds as \(n^2\). At \(n=20\) the iid sd of the total is 2.5× too small → 50% coverage.

Fix (the shipped model). Give the residual its persistence, one parameter:

\[ e_t = \rho\, e_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \hat\rho = 0.54\ [0.21,\,0.87] \]
  • An AR(1) likelihood ≡ GLS re-weighting of the pre-period. It moves the estimate (€229k → €262k, beside the classical €260k), re-prices the width (2.5×), and lifts coverage 50% → 88%, level with the referee's 83%.
  • How bad was the first model, concretely? Its 90% interval is [€{{nb07.iid_lo}}k, €{{nb07.iid_hi}}k] — it excludes the planted truth of €{{nb07.true_total}}k. On one dataset it would have looked perfectly well-behaved: clean fit, tight interval, wrong.

The transferable rule (Bertrand et al. 2004, in Bayesian dress)
Serially correlated errors, summed over time, destroy naive uncertainty; it cost the diff-in-diff literature a decade. The rule is not "always AR(1)" (its ACF decays; the data's is flat; it matches the variance of the sum, not the shape; a local-level model à la CausalImpact matches the shape). The rule is: when a posterior feeds a decision about a sum, check its coverage against a design-based referee. None of this changed the verdict; it was banked in Act IV.

Advanced note

The more precise route we did not take

AR(1) matched the variance of the sum, not the shape of the error. The flat autocorrelation really points at a near-integrated, local-level error: a random-walk level, matched by construction in a structural time-series model (Brodersen et al. 2015, CausalImpact):

\[ Y_{1t}=\mu_t+\textstyle\sum_j w_j Y_{jt}+\varepsilon_t, \qquad \mu_t=\mu_{t-1}+\eta_t \] a latent level \(\mu_t\) fitted jointly with the donor blend: a state-space model
\[ \mathrm{Var}\Bigl(\textstyle\sum_t e_t\Bigr):\quad \underbrace{n}_{\text{iid}} \;<\; \underbrace{n^2}_{\text{offset}} \;<\; \underbrace{n^3}_{\text{random walk}} \]
  • The truth sits between \(n^2\) and \(n^3\); AR(1) prices the sum, not the shape.
Why we stopped at AR(1)
On a 20-week window AR(1) already lands the total's scale and covers at 88%; the extra state-space machinery buys shape-correctness that does not move this verdict. Over a much longer post-period, where the \(n^2\)-vs-\(n^3\) gap widens, this is where you would go.
