Act I · The question

The boardroom question

A campaign ran in one region. Sales rose. Marketing wants €4M to go national. You decide.

What you have:

  • Weekly sales for 30 metro markets, 60 weeks.
  • In week 40, exactly one metro switched on a €{{nb07.cost}}k campaign for 20 weeks.
  • The other 29 did nothing. No randomisation — the metro was chosen.
  • Gross margin on incremental sales: {{nb07.margin}}%.

The question is not "did sales go up?" It is three questions, in order:
① how much did the campaign cause · ② is that more than luck · ③ is the profit worth the price?

Why this lecture exists
This is the single most common causal problem in marketing analytics: one treated unit, observational data, an aggregate time series. No A/B test is possible after the fact. Synthetic control is the field's standard answer — it is what Meta's GeoLift, Google's geo methodology, and every serious geo-testing vendor run underneath.
Today's two punchlines
① A campaign can be statistically real and commercially worthless — the margin trap.
② A model's 90% interval can be right half the time — and you can catch it.

The model that generated what you just saw

Act II · The method — causal foundations

Causal inference is a missing-data problem

Write the treated metro's sales in week t two ways: \(Y_{0t}(1)\) with the campaign, \(Y_{0t}(0)\) without it. The causal effect is the difference between two worlds:

\[ \tau_t \;=\; Y_{0t}(1) \,-\, Y_{0t}(0), \qquad \tau \;=\; \sum_{t \geq T_0} \tau_t \] per-week effect, and the estimand the business buys: the 20-week total

After launch we observe \(Y_{0t}(1)\). We never observe \(Y_{0t}(0)\) — the fundamental problem of causal inference. Every method in this course is a different way to fill that missing cell.

Define the estimand first
Per-week effect and 20-week total are different estimands with different uncertainties. The rollout decision turns on the total — remember this: it is exactly where the Bayesian model will later break.

Why DiD is not enough here
Difference-in-differences removes the level gap but assumes parallel trends — every market responds identically to the shared shocks. Metros differ in exposure to the macro cycle (different industries, demographics), so their trends are not parallel. We need a control with the same exposures as the treated metro.
Act II · The method — the estimator

Abadie's idea: if no twin exists, build one

Assume untreated sales follow an interactive fixed-effects (factor) structure — the DGP you saw is exactly one, with \(\lambda_t = f_t\) and \(\mu_j = (\alpha_j, \gamma_j)\):

\[ Y_{jt}(0) \;=\; \delta_t \;+\; \lambda_t^{\top} \mu_j \;+\; \varepsilon_{jt} \] δt common shock · λt latent factors · μj market j's loadings — none observed

A blend with weights w has loadings \(\sum_j w_j \mu_j\). If the weights match the treated metro's loadings,

\[ \sum_j w_j\, \mu_j \;\approx\; \mu_0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \sum_j w_j\, Y_{jt} \;\approx\; Y_{0t}(0) \;\; \forall t \] match the loadings ⟹ the blend reproduces the missing counterfactual — through shocks that haven't happened yet

We cannot see the loadings \(\mu\) — but we can see their fingerprint: the pre-period trajectory. A blend that tracks the treated metro through 40 weeks of shocks has, with high probability, matched its exposures.

The key intuition
DiD matches a level. Synthetic control matches the response to shocks — that is why it survives non-parallel trends that break DiD.
Pedigree
Abadie & Gardeazabal (2003), Abadie, Diamond & Hainmueller (2010) — "arguably the most important innovation in the policy-evaluation literature in the last 15 years" (Athey & Imbens). The same machinery now prices ad campaigns.
Act II · The method — the estimator

The estimator, precisely

Fit the weights on the pre-period only (\(t < T_0 = 40\)), constrained to the simplex:

\[ \hat{w} \;=\; \operatorname*{argmin}_{w \in \Delta} \; \sum_{t < T_0} \left( Y_{0t} - \sum_j w_j Y_{jt} \right)^{2}, \qquad \Delta = \{\, w : w_j \geq 0,\; \sum_j w_j = 1 \,\} \] constrained least squares on the simplex — solved by SLSQP; no likelihood, no prior

Read the effect off the post-period gap, and summarise the blend's concentration:

\[ \hat{\tau}_t = Y_{0t} - \sum_j \hat{w}_j Y_{jt}, \qquad \hat{\tau} = \sum_{t \geq T_0} \hat{\tau}_t, \qquad n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1 \,/\, \sum_j \hat{w}_j^{\,2} = {{nb07.cl_eff_donors}} \] treated − synthetic, summed over 20 post weeks · neff = inverse Herfindahl of the weights

Why the simplex constraint? It forces interpolation, never extrapolation: no "−80% of Milan + 190% of Rome". Three consequences:

  • Interpretability — "our metro behaves like 40% dma_08 + 32% dma_20 + …" is a sentence a media planner can act on.
  • Regularisation — the solution is sparse: {{nb07.cl_eff_donors}} effective donors out of 29.
  • Honesty about reach — a market outside its donors' convex hull cannot be matched, and the fit will say so.
A measured caution (from the notebook)
Unconstrained OLS fits the pre-period better ({{nb07.rmspe_unconstrained}} vs {{nb07.rmspe_simplex}}) and predicts worse out of sample ({{nb07.hull_oos_ratio}}×). The constraint is a guarantee about the object — a defensible interpolation — not a promise to win every panel.

Act II · The method — identification

What has to be true for the gap to be causal

Estimation is arithmetic; identification is the argument. Four assumptions, each with a check.
① Pre-fit quality
The blend must track the treated metro pre-launch: \( \sqrt{ \frac{1}{T_0} \sum_{t<T_0} \hat{\tau}_t^{\,2} } \;<\; \hat{\tau}_{\mathrm{wk}} / 3 \) — RMSE under a third of the weekly lift. Check: here {{nb07.cl_pre_rmse}} vs a bar of {{nb07.gate_ref}}: pass.
② No anticipation
Sales must not react before the campaign (stock-piling, pre-buying, leaked launch). Check: placebo-in-time — back-date the launch 10 weeks; the "effect" in the fake window is ≈ €0.
③ No spillover (SUTVA)
The campaign must not touch donor markets (media bleed, travel, e-commerce). Check: impossible from data alone — a design duty. Simulation: leaked spillover makes SC under-state the lift, never inflate it.
④ Convex hull & stable loadings
The treated metro must be reproducible as a mix of donors, with exposures stable through the window. Check: pre-fit again + leave-one-out donor robustness (estimate moves only €{{nb07.loo_lo}}–{{nb07.loo_hi}}k).
The referee principle
①, ② and ④ are testable and the notebook tests them. ③ is not — which is why geo-test design (separating test and control regions) matters more than any statistic computed afterwards.
Act III · Inference without a likelihood

Is €{{nb07.cl_total}}k more than luck?

There is no standard error here — no likelihood was written, and with one treated unit the usual asymptotics have nothing to bite on. So how do we test significance?

✋ Poll
We let each of the 30 markets take a turn as the "treated" one and re-measure the effect each time. Where does the real treated metro rank among the 30?
A — rank {{nb07.treated_rank}} of 30. And that rank is the inference: if the campaign did nothing, the treated metro would be exchangeable with the donors, so P(rank 1 by luck) = 1/30 ≈ {{nb07.p_ratio}}. You have just re-derived the permutation test.
Act III · Inference without a likelihood

Placebo-in-space: the null you can hold in your hand

Refit the estimator on every donor as if it were treated. Their 29 "effects" are draws from the null — no campaign anywhere.

\[ p \;=\; \frac{ 1 + \#\{\, j : |\hat{\tau}_j| \geq |\hat{\tau}_0| \,\} }{ J + 1 }, \qquad p_{\min} = \frac{1}{J+1} = \frac{1}{30} \approx {{nb07.p_ratio}} \] exact finite-sample inference under exchangeability of the treated unit with its donors — no asymptotics, no likelihood

Here the real gap outranks all placebos: p = {{nb07.p_ratio}}, at the floor. The effect is real.

Design lesson
The granularity of your evidence is set by the number of control markets, not by the weeks of data. Small donor pools make significance literally unattainable.

Act III · Inference without a likelihood

From a test to an interval: inversion

A p-value is a yes/no. Decisions need "how big, ± what". The same placebo cloud yields an interval: a hypothesised lift H survives when the real gap, corrected for H, looks like an ordinary placebo draw.

\[ \mathrm{RI}_{90} \;=\; \{\, H : H \;\text{not rejected at 10\%} \,\} \;=\; \left[\, \hat{\tau} - q_{0.95}, \;\; \hat{\tau} - q_{0.05} \,\right] \] q = quantiles of the placebo totals · assumes a constant additive effect under the null · result: [€{{nb07.ri_lo}}k, €{{nb07.ri_hi}}k]
Why this interval is special
It inverts a permutation test, so it never assumed normality, independence, or any error structure. It measured its own noise. Later, a model that does assume structure will be graded against it — keep it as the referee.

Act V · The probability — Bayesian synthetic control

The decision is made. One number is missing.

The classical arm has already decided: the profit interval straddles the cost → not a confident buy. That verdict is banked — nothing that follows will move it.

But "the interval straddles" is a yes/no fact. A board allocating capital across campaigns asks a sharper question:

\[ \mathbb{P}\left( \mathrm{profit} > c \right) \;=\; \mathbb{P}\left( m\,\tau > c \right) \;=\; \int \mathbf{1}\{ m\,\tau > c \}\; p(\tau \mid \mathrm{data})\, d\tau \] a p-value cannot produce this — it scores a procedure under a null, not a bet about τ; the integral needs a posterior

Only a posterior distribution over \(\tau\) yields that number. That is precisely — and only — what the Bayesian layer adds.

The sequencing contract
Decision from the classical arm; probability from the posterior. Because the verdict is already safe, we can be ruthlessly honest about the model's defects in the next act — including one that would have been embarrassing if the decision had depended on it.
Same identification, new arithmetic
The Bayesian refit uses the same donors, the same simplex, the same estimand. The causal work was done by the design. The sampler adds a distribution — not causality.
Act V · The probability — Bayesian synthetic control

The model, in full

\[ w \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\mathbf{1}_{29}), \qquad \sigma \sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(5) \]
\[ Y_{0t} \mid w, \sigma \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\left( \sum_j w_j Y_{jt}, \;\; \sigma^2 \right), \qquad t < T_0 \] the Dirichlet lives on the simplex — the hard constraint becomes a prior; fitted on the pre-period only
with pm.Model():
    w  = pm.Dirichlet("w", a=np.ones(29))   # weights on the simplex
    sd = pm.HalfNormal("sd", 5)            # blend-error scale (€k)
    mu = pm.math.dot(w, donors_pre)         # the synthetic control
    pm.Normal("y", mu, sd, observed=treated_pre)
    idata = pm.sample()                     # NUTS

What the posterior buys, mechanically: every draw of w is a full counterfactual path, so every draw is a value of \(\tau\) — the estimate arrives with its own distribution.

  • Prior robustness (checked): sweeping the Dirichlet concentration \(\alpha \in \{0.5, 1, 2\}\) barely moves the total — 40 pre-weeks swamp the prior.
  • A real trade on the weights: the flat Dirichlet spreads the blend over {{nb07.bayes_eff_donors}} effective donors vs the classical {{nb07.cl_eff_donors}} — more stable out of sample, less interpretable to a planner.
One word in the likelihood is doing enormous work
The weekly errors are assumed independent. It is the default of every regression ever written, and it is invisible on the slide above. Hold that thought.

Act VI · Honest uncertainty — diagnosis

Why the sum breaks when the weeks don't

The blend never matches the treated metro's loadings \(\gamma_0\) exactly. The unmatched share of the macro random walk \(m_t\) leaks into the gap — not as fresh weekly noise, but as a persistent offset \(\omega\): one draw of "how well did this donor pool match?", held for the whole window.

\[ e_t \;=\; \omega + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \omega \sim (0, \sigma_\omega^2) \;\text{once per world}, \quad \varepsilon_t \;\text{fresh each week} \] the gap error, decomposed — ω is the unmatched slice of the shared wave

Now sum n = 20 weeks and watch the variance:

\[ \mathrm{Var}\left( \sum_{t=1}^{n} e_t \right) \;=\; n^2 \sigma_\omega^2 + n\, \sigma_\varepsilon^2 \qquad \text{vs the iid claim:} \quad n\,(\sigma_\omega^2 + \sigma_\varepsilon^2) \] the offset compounds as n²; the iid model charges n — at n = 20 that is a {{nb07.total_sd_ratio}}× under-pricing of the sd of the total

The independence assumption pins \(\sigma_\omega\) to zero. Consequences, measured on the 24 panels:

Quantityiid model claimsReality

The general lesson (Bertrand et al. 2004, in Bayesian dress)
Serially correlated errors, summed over time, destroy naive standard errors. It cost the diff-in-diff literature a decade of overstated significance. Same lesson, new likelihood.

Act VI · Honest uncertainty — repair

The fix: give the likelihood the persistence

\[ e_t \;=\; \rho\, e_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) \] one new parameter; same flat Dirichlet on w; the post-period error is simulated forward, not redrawn
\[ \hat{w}_{\mathrm{GLS}} \;=\; \operatorname*{argmin}_{w}\; (y - Dw)^{\top} \Sigma_\rho^{-1} (y - Dw) \] an AR(1) likelihood is GLS: Σρ re-weights which pre-period weeks the fit trusts — that is why the estimate moves, not just the width

The posterior finds \(\rho\) = {{nb07.rho_post}} [{{nb07.rho_lo}}, {{nb07.rho_hi}}] — the quantity the iid model had silently pinned to zero. Three things happen:

  • The estimate moves: €{{nb07.iid_total}}k → €{{nb07.bayes_total}}k. An AR(1) likelihood is a GLS re-weighting of the pre-period — it changes which weeks the fit trusts, hence the weights, hence the counterfactual. Likelihoods touch centres, not just widths.
  • The interval re-prices: {{nb07.width_ratio}}× wider.
  • It earns the width: total coverage {{nb07.total_cov}}% → {{nb07.total_cov_arone}}%, level with the referee's {{nb07.ri_cov}}%.
The honest caveat — said out loud
AR(1) is, in one respect, the wrong family: its ACF decays; the data's is flat. What it matches is not the ACF's shape but the variance of the sum — \(\rho\) inflates until the 20-week total is priced right. Fine here; over a much longer window you'd fit a local-level (random-walk) state-space model that matches the shape by construction (Brodersen et al. 2015 — CausalImpact).
The transferable rule
Not "always use AR(1)". It is: when a posterior's interval under-covers, suspect the likelihood — and check coverage against a design-based referee.

The iid posterior is the outlier: it underprices the fresh-panel spread by {{nb07.underprice}}×. A standard deviation two-and-a-bit times too small is precisely a band that is right half the time.

The placebo scale is instructive the other way. At €{{nb07.placebo_sd}}k it also sits below the €{{nb07.seed_sd}}k yardstick, yet its interval covers at {{nb07.ri_cov}}%: it inverts placebo quantiles, so it needs roughly the right shape, not exactly the right scale.

The asymmetry that decides everything
Being roughly right about the spread costs efficiency. Being wrong about the structure of the errors costs validity. The iid likelihood commits to a false structure, so its interval is both too narrow and too rarely right.
Act VI · Honest uncertainty — the mechanism

n, n², n³: what an error's shape does to a sum

\[ e_t = \omega + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \mathrm{Var}\!\Big(\sum_{t=1}^{n} e_t\Big) = n^2\,\sigma_\omega^2 + n\,\sigma_\varepsilon^2 \] one persistent offset ω per world, held for the whole window, on top of fresh weekly noise; the offset term compounds as n², not n
  • Independent errors: the sum's variance grows as \(n\). That is the iid bet.
  • Constant offset: \(n^2\). Its autocorrelation is flat: a level shift is equally present at every lag, exactly the ACF the placebo worlds measured.
  • Near-integrated random walk: \(n^3\). The truth here sits between \(n^2\) and \(n^3\).

AR(1) matches none of these shapes: its ACF decays geometrically, the decay the data refused. What it prices is the sum: ρ is pulled up until the summed variance comes out right at this window length.

When the shortcut stops working
AR(1) lands the total's scale at \(n = {{nb07.n_post}}\) weeks. Much longer windows force the honest route: a random-walk or local-level state-space component that matches the shape itself, not just this one sum.

Act VII · What can go wrong — spillover

The violation no placebo can see

If the campaign leaks into neighbouring donor markets, every placebo refit is contaminated in exactly the same way. Nothing looks unusual. Only a simulator can plant the violation and watch.
\[ \text{bias} \;\approx\; -\,\varphi \sum_{j \in \text{nbrs}} w_j \,\times\, \tau \] leaked lift raises the contaminated donors, so the synthetic rises and the measured gap shrinks by roughly the spilled weight

Planted in the simulator: the formula predicts a bias of {{nb07.sp_predicted}} €k; the refit measures {{nb07.sp_measured}} €k. The mechanism is understood, not just observed.

Fix by design, not statistics
Spillover attenuates: it understates a real campaign, never inflates it. That is conservative for a GO, though it can turn a true GO into a NO-GO. Since no placebo can flag it, drop adjacent and media-overlapping markets from the donor pool before fitting anything. A smaller clean pool beats a larger contaminated one.

Act IX · The verdict

What transfers beyond this case

1 · The estimator is the identification strategy
Before/after, DiD, synthetic control differ only in what they assume about the missing Y(0). Choose the assumption you can defend, not the formula you remember.
2 · Match responses to shocks, not levels
Synthetic control's edge over DiD: simplex weights fit the treated unit's factor exposures, so the twin survives non-parallel trends.
3 · With one treated unit, rank — don't t-test
Placebo permutation gives exact finite-sample inference with no likelihood; inverting it gives the interval. Floor: \(1/(J+1)\) — donor count is your power.
4 · Profit = margin × lift − cost
The break-even iROAS is 1/margin, not 1. Statistical significance and commercial worthiness are different questions asked of different objects.
5 · Decide on probabilities, not means
A right-skewed posterior can put the mean above cost while most of the mass is below it. P(profit > c) is the number a board should sign.
6 · Calibrate before you integrate
A 90% interval that covers {{nb07.total_cov}}% of the time poisons every number computed from it. When a posterior under-covers, suspect the likelihood (here: iid errors vs a persistent offset) — and keep a design-based referee.
