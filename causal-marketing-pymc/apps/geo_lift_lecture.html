    Did the regional campaign work?

    From one metro's sales bump to a national-rollout decision — and the two numbers that decide it.

    1 The boardroom question

    A brand ran a paid campaign in one region for eight weeks. Sales there rose. Marketing wants 4M to take it national. You have to say yes or no.

    The setup is the everyday one. You have weekly sales for 30 metro markets. In week 40 exactly one of them — call it the treated metro — switched on a 90k campaign. The other 29 did nothing. Twenty weeks later, the treated metro's sales sit visibly higher than before.

    The business question is not "did sales go up?" It is: how much of that rise did the campaign cause, is it more than luck, and is the profit worth more than the price? Everything today serves those three sub-questions, in that order.

    ▶ LIVE The data you actually get
    Thirty markets (grey), one treated (dark), campaign at the orange line. Tick the box to strip out the average across all markets — watch how much of the treated "bump" was just the tide lifting every boat.
    The trap
    The treated metro's own before-vs-after difference is /week. But the campaign's true effect is 14.2k/week. The gap is the shared wave — seasonality, the economy, a category trend — that moved every market. Before/after books that wave as if the campaign caused it.

    2 Why the obvious answer is wrong

    Four analysts, four shortcuts, one honest answer. Each is a different identification strategy — a different assumption about what the counterfactual "no-campaign" sales would have been.

    ✋ Poll · put a number on it
    The treated metro's sales are 12% higher after launch than before. Your gut: what share of that 12% was actually caused by the campaign?
    C. On this panel the campaign is worth about 14.2k/week against a pre-launch level near 120k — roughly 2–3 points of the 12%. The rest is the macro wave. Naive before/after (/week) is off because it silently attributes the whole tide to the boat that happened to run an ad.

    The one idea
    The estimator is the assumption. To measure the campaign you need a credible counterfactual: what the treated metro would have sold with no campaign. The rest of the lecture is one good way to build it.

    3 Build a synthetic twin

    If no single market is a good control, build one — a weighted blend of the untreated markets that tracked the treated one before the campaign.

    Pick weights w₁…w₂₉ on the 29 donor markets, non-negative and summing to one, so the blend Σⱼ wⱼ·(donor j) hugs the treated metro through the pre-period. If it tracks before launch, it is the best available stand-in for "no campaign" after launch. The post-launch gap is the estimated lift.

    ▶ LIVE Fit the blend yourself — can you beat the optimiser?
    Sliders are the four donors the optimiser leans on. The dashed blue line is your synthetic; the shaded band after week 40 is the implied campaign lift. Minimise the pre-fit error — the optimiser (SLSQP) reaches 3.23.
    Definition · the simplex
    Weights that are non-negative and sum to one live on the simplex. This forces the synthetic to interpolate among real markets, never extrapolate — no "-80% of Milan plus 190% of Rome". You reconstruct the treated metro from a convex mix of markets that actually exist.
    ▸ 📐 How the weights are actually fitted (SLSQP)

    The weights minimise pre-period squared error subject to two constraints — wⱼ ≥ 0 and Σwⱼ = 1 — a constrained least-squares problem solved by sequential quadratic programming (SLSQP). It is deterministic: no sampler, no priors. One subtlety the book flags: a naive solver can silently return its equal-weight starting point and report "success", so the pre-fit gate below is also a convergence check.

    4 The precondition: earn the right to read the gap

    A synthetic twin is only trustworthy if it tracked the treated metro before the campaign, when the true effect is zero. That is the pre-fit gate: the pre-period fit error must be small relative to the weekly lift you hope to detect. Fail it, and the post-launch gap means nothing.

    Gate
    On this case the classical fit tracks to a pre-RMSE of 3.2k — comfortably under the bar. The synthetic hugs zero before launch and departs after: no systematic mis-fit, just the shape a good control makes. Only now may we read the post-launch gap as lift.
    ▸ 📐 Why this gate has real teeth on live data

    On the real Google geo experiment in the book, the country's largest market lies outside the convex hull of its donors — no blend can match it — so the pre-fit fails loudly and the method refuses to ship an estimate at all. That refusal is the gate working. A method that always returns a number is more dangerous than one that sometimes says "I can't."

    5 Is it real, or a lucky metro?

    The gap is 260k over 20 weeks. But some metro was always going to look most "lifted" by chance. We need a null: how big a gap do markets show when nothing happened?

    The trick needs no formula. Pretend each of the 29 donors was the treated one, refit the synthetic, and measure its placebo "effect". That gives 29 gaps from worlds with no campaign. Rank the real gap against them — a permutation test.

    ✋ Poll · guess the rank
    We let all 30 markets take turns being "treated". Our real treated metro's measured effect — where does it rank among the 30?
    A — rank #1 of 30. The real gap is more extreme than every placebo, so the permutation p-value is 1/30 ≈ 0.033. That is the finite-sample floor: with 29 donors you cannot get below 1/30 no matter how large the effect. The effect is real. Notice what this did not tell you: whether it was worth the money.
    ▶ LIVE The placebo null the real gap is ranked against
    Grey dots: the 20-week total gap for each donor pretending to be treated (true effect zero). Green: the real treated metro. It sits clean outside the placebo cloud — rank #1, p ≈ 0.033.
    ▸ 📐 The permutation p-value and its floor

    With J donors the permutation p-value is (number of placebo effects at least as extreme, +1) / (J+1). The "+1" counts the real unit itself; it makes the smallest attainable p exactly 1/(J+1) = 1/30 ≈ 0.033. This is why geo tests want many candidate control markets: more donors, a finer null, a lower floor.

    6 From "real" to "how big, give or take"

    A yes/no is not a decision input. We need an interval on the total lift — without writing down a likelihood we might get wrong.

    The same placebo null gives it. A candidate total H is not rejected when the real gap minus H looks like an ordinary placebo draw. Sweep H; the values you cannot reject form the randomization interval. Drag the hypothesis below and watch it flip from "rejected" to "inside the interval".

    ▶ LIVE An interval by inverting the placebo test
    Bottom lane: the placebo cloud around zero. Top lane: shift it by H. When the real gap (green) sits inside the shifted cloud, H is not rejected. The full non-rejected range is [195k, 335k].
    The one interval everything downstream uses
    Point estimate 260k, 90% randomization interval [195k, 335k]. No likelihood, no prior — it inverts a permutation test, so it cannot be wrong about the error structure. Keep this interval in view; the whole decision flows from it.

    7 The margin trap

    Here is where most rollout decisions go wrong. The effect is real and large. Watch it evaporate.

    ✋ Poll · do the arithmetic
    The campaign cost 90k and drove 260k of extra sales. Gross margin is 35%. What is the profit on the campaign?
    C — roughly 1k net. Revenue is not profit. The firm keeps 35% of the 260k lift = 91k of gross profit, and the campaign cost 90k — so it net cleared the price by about 1k. Answer A (170k) is the seductive error: it forgets the campaign only earns the margin on incremental sales, not the whole top line.
    ▶ LIVE Where the money goes
    A waterfall: 260k of lift, times the margin, minus the 90k cost. At 35% the campaign barely breaks even. Slide the margin — see how narrow the winning zone is.
    The break-even you must beat
    iROAS on this campaign is 2.89 — every 1 of spend returned 2.89 of sales, which sounds like a triumph. But at a 35% margin the break-even iROAS is 1/0.35 = 2.86. 2.89 versus 2.86. It "worked" — and it barely paid.
    ▸ 📐 iROAS, break-even, and the profit identity

    Profit = margin × lift − cost. Break-even lift = cost / margin = 90k / 0.35 = 257k. Break-even iROAS = 1 / margin = 2.86. A campaign pays iff its lift clears 257k — and our estimate is 260k, a hair above. This is why "iROAS > 1" is a marketing vanity metric: the bar is not 1, it is 1/margin.

    8 The decision — and how fragile it is

    At 90k the point estimate says "just barely pays". But the interval on profit runs from 68k to 117k — it straddles the 90k cost. A comfortable-looking mean the interval refuses to bless. Slide the price and watch the verdict move.

    9 What a probability adds

    A board reasons in probabilities: P(profit > cost). A p-value cannot give one — it scores a procedure, not a bet on the effect. The posterior can.

    Refit the same synthetic control as a Bayesian model — same donors, same simplex, now with a distribution over the weights. It reaches the same estimate (the causal work was done by the design, not the sampler) and adds one thing: the full distribution of profit.

    ▶ LIVE Mean says yes, probability says no
    Posterior of profit (35% of lift). Green mass is profit > price. Expected profit is 92k — above the 90k cost — yet P(profit>cost) = 0.45, below a coin flip, because the distribution is right-skewed. The mean is flattered by a thin fat tail.
    Why the rule reads a probability, not a mean
    A mean pulled above the cost line by its upper tail would wave through a campaign that, more likely than not, loses money. The verdict is NO-GO: statistically significant (p = 0.033) and commercially a coin-flip-losing bet at this price.

    10 Can you trust that 90% interval?

    The probability is only as honest as the interval behind it. Here is the subtlest — and most important — idea in the chapter.

    ✋ Poll · what does "90%" deliver?
    The first Bayesian model reports a 90% interval for the total lift. We check it on 24 fresh simulated worlds where we know the true answer. In how many does the interval actually contain the truth?
    C — 12 of 24, about 50%. A "90%" interval that is right half the time is a broken instrument — and it is broken about exactly the number the business buys, the 20-week total. Its per-week bands are fine (86%). The failure is in the sum. Why? Read on.

    The blend's weekly error is not independent. Whatever slice of the shared macro wave the weights fail to match sits as a persistent offset across the whole post-period — one draw of "how well did this pool match?", held for all 20 weeks. Summing 20 correlated weeks compounds it instead of averaging it away. The independence assumption prices that at zero.

    ▶ LIVE The tell: the error's autocorrelation is flat, not decaying
    Grey bars: the measured autocorrelation of the gap error — flat near 0.3 out to eight weeks. The blue curve is what an AR(1) model with correlation ρ implies: it decays. Slide ρ up and it still vanishes by lag 8 — it never goes flat. The fix (AR(1)) matches the variance of the sum, not the shape.

    Put the persistence into the likelihood and the interval widens by 2.5× — and its coverage jumps from 50% to 88%, level with the design-based interval's 83%. One extra parameter, no new data, buys an interval a board can trust.

    ▶ LIVE Two posteriors of the same total — one honest, one not
    Orange: the independent-errors posterior — narrow, and it misses the truth (dashed). Blue: the persistence-aware AR(1) posterior — wider, and it covers. Same estimate, very different confidence.
    ▸ 📐 Corroboration, not recovery — the two-blends-one-total point

    The AR(1) fix moves the estimate from 229k up to 262k, which lands beside the classical 260k — but by a completely different weight profile. The classical blend leans on 3.3 effective donors; the Bayesian one spreads over 23.4. Two estimators that disagree on which markets to trust, yet agree on the total, are harder to dismiss than either alone. That is corroboration across weightings — a stronger result than one method reproducing the other.

    ▸ 📐 Why the variance of the sum blows up: n vs n² vs n³

    Independent errors give the total a variance that grows like n. A constant offset gives n². A near-integrated random-walk level gives n³. The flat autocorrelation points at that last, local-level case, so the true variance sits between n² and n³ — while the independent likelihood charges only n. AR(1) prices none of the shapes exactly; it inflates ρ until the summed variance is right on this 20-week window. For a much longer post-period you would reach for a structural time-series (local-level) model that matches the shape by construction.

    11 The verdict — and what you actually do Monday

    Every arm the chapter fits says the same thing: not a national rollout on this evidence. The label moves between NO-GO and TEST FURTHER only because the campaign sits on the break-even line — the honest signature of a marginal campaign. The action never changes.

    ✋ Poll · buy more certainty?
    It is a NO-GO, but close. You could run a bigger confirmatory geo test. Roughly how many test markets would it take to reach 90% confidence that this pays at 90k?
    C. Because the profit sits a whisker above cost, driving P(profit>cost) above 0.9 would need the standard deviation shrunk to a sliver — more independent markets than exist. The value of perfect information here is only about 8k: perfect certainty would rarely overturn the call, so a study costing many times that is not caution, it is waste. The move is to renegotiate the price, not buy more data.
    The deliverable to the board
    Not "the campaign failed". Rather: "at a 35% margin this evidence supports a confident buy only up to 67k (the headroom price); the asking price was 90k. Renegotiate, extend the test window, or accept a lower confidence bar with open eyes — but do not roll it out nationally blind."

    The whole argument in one breath

    Before/after lies because a shared wave moves every market → build a synthetic twin from a simplex-weighted donor blend that tracked the treated metro pre-launch → a placebo permutation shows the effect is real (p = 0.033) → inverting that placebo gives an honest interval → the margin trap turns a 2.89 iROAS into a €1k net → Bayes states it as P(profit>cost) = 0.45 → but only after the persistence-aware fix makes that interval honest → verdict: NO-GO, and the real deliverable is the price at which it would be a go.
