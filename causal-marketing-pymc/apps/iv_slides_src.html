Act I · The question

The boardroom question

A demand-side platform wants your bid cap raised. Exposed users convert far more. You sign the number.

What you have:

  • A retailer buys display-ad exposures through a demand-side platform (DSP).
  • The dashboard: users who saw an ad convert far more. Naive gap: €{{nb11.naive}} of sales per exposure.
  • The catch: the platform chose whom to expose, and it chose people already likely to buy. No experiment on who sees the ad.
  • Each exposure costs €{{nb11.cost}}. Raise the cap, or not?

The question is not "do the exposed buy more?" It is three questions, in order:
1. how much did an exposure cause · 2. is that more than luck · 3. is it worth more than the price?

Why this lecture exists
This is endogenous exposure: the treatment chose itself. It is the most common causal problem in performance marketing, ad measurement, and pricing. No back-door adjustment saves you, because the thing that drove exposure (intent) is exactly what you cannot see.
Today's two punchlines
1. Most of the dashboard gap is intent, not advertising: the number finance wants to bid on is the wrong number.
2. A weak instrument is worse than none: it can be precisely wrong.

For each user you see three things: whether the lottery nudged them (\(Z\)), whether they were exposed (\(X\)), and their sales (\(Y\)). You do not see their buying intent (\(U\)), the very thing that drove exposure.

Act I · The question · the world behind the data

The model that generated what you just saw

A simulation, which is a feature: we know the truth, so every method today can be graded. Four lines are the whole world.
\[ U \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1), \qquad Z \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.5) \] \(U\) = buying intent, unobserved · \(Z\) = a randomized serving-priority lottery, the instrument
\[ \Pr(X{=}1) \;=\; \sigma\!\left(\alpha_0 + \gamma\,Z + \lambda\,U\right) \] exposure is driven by the lottery \(\gamma Z\) and by intent \(\lambda U\): this is the endogeneity
\[ Y \;=\; \mu + \underbrace{\beta}_{\text{€15 truth}} X \;+\; \underbrace{\kappa\,U}_{\text{confound}} \;+\; \varepsilon \] sales = the ad effect \(\beta\) we want, plus intent \(\kappa U\) leaking straight into \(Y\)
Read it by what breaks
\(U\) sits in both the exposure equation and the sales equation. That single shared cause is why the naive gap is wrong, and why no adjustment on observed variables can fix it: you cannot control for what you cannot measure.
The one lever we do control
The lottery \(Z\) enters only the exposure equation, and it is assigned by a coin. It moves \(X\) without touching \(U\). That is the crack the whole method pries open.

The plan: use only the part of exposure that the lottery moved, because intent could not have caused a coin flip.

Act II · The method · causal foundations

Causal inference is a missing-data problem

Write a user's sales two ways: \(Y_i(1)\) if exposed, \(Y_i(0)\) if not. The effect on that user is the difference between two worlds:

\[ \tau_i \;=\; Y_i(1) \,-\, Y_i(0) \] the causal effect of one exposure, for user \(i\)

You observe \(Y_i(1)\) for the exposed and \(Y_i(0)\) for the unexposed. You never observe both for the same person. That is the fundamental problem of causal inference: the counterfactual is missing.

Why the naive gap fails
It swaps the missing cell for the wrong people: it uses the unexposed as the exposed's \(Y(0)\). But the platform picked the exposed for their intent, so the two groups had different \(Y(0)\) to begin with.
\(Y(0)\) no ad\(Y(1)\) ad
exposed usersmissingobserved
unexposed usersobservedmissing

Two of the four cells are missing. The naive estimator pretends the unexposed row fills the exposed row's gap. It does not: the rows are different people, sorted by intent.

"What would the exposed have bought with no ad?" is an estimation target, not a rhetorical question.

Act II · The method · the general problem

Confounding is a back-door path

A confounder \(U\) opens a back-door path from treatment to outcome: \(X \leftarrow U \rightarrow Y\). Association flows along it even when \(X\) does nothing, so the raw correlation mixes cause with sorting.

The usual fix is to close the back door by conditioning on \(U\):

\[ \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X, U] \;=\; \mu + \beta X + \kappa U \] hold intent fixed and the \(\beta\) that remains is causal
Why it fails here
You cannot condition on a variable you never measured. Intent is unlogged. Every back-door method (regression controls, matching, propensity scores, DML) needs \(U\) in the data. When the confounder is unobserved, they are all stuck.
the back door, and the front way in
Top: the confounded fork. An instrument \(Z\) (bottom) does not close the back door, it goes around it: it injects variation into \(X\) that \(U\) could not have caused.

Act II · The method · the idea

An instrument is a lever on exposure that intent could not have moved

The case's instrument \(Z\) is a randomized serving-priority lottery: on a coin flip the platform bumps a user up the auction queue, raising the chance they see the ad. Three conditions make it work:

1. Relevance
\(Z\) actually moves exposure. testable it is the first-stage \(F\).
2. Exogeneity
\(Z\) is unrelated to intent \(U\). by design it is a coin.
3. Exclusion
\(Z\) touches \(Y\) only through \(X\). untestable argued, never checked.
The crack it pries open
Because the coin is blind to intent, any sales change it produces must have travelled through exposure. That is a slice of \(X\) with no \(U\) attached, and it is all we need.
\[ Z \;\perp\; U, \qquad Z \not\rightarrow Y \mid X \] random and content-free: the two claims the whole estimate rests on
Not a control variable
An instrument is the opposite of a covariate. You do not condition on \(Z\); you use its variation. A good control blocks a path; a good instrument opens one.
Act II · The method · the estimand

The instrument speaks for the compliers, and no one else

▶ LIVE the base, split by response to the nudge
Push \(\gamma\): the complier slice (green) grows. It is the first stage. A stronger instrument speaks for a larger share of the base.

Sort users by how they react to the nudge:

  • Always-takers: exposed regardless.
  • Never-takers: never exposed.
  • Compliers: exposed only because the lottery nudged them.

When the coin flips, always- and never-takers do not move, so they cancel. Only compliers respond, so the instrument measures the effect on compliers: the LATE.

Not a bug, the right target
Compliers are exactly the users a higher bid would newly expose. LATE is the number a bid decision needs. We rule out defiers (monotonicity): the nudge never pushes anyone away from exposure.
Act II · The method · the estimator

The estimator is three regressions and one division

\[ \pi \;=\; \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z{=}1] - \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z{=}0] \] first stage: how much the nudge moved exposure. Here \({{nb11.fs_lo}}\% \rightarrow {{nb11.fs_hi}}\%\), so \(\pi \approx {{nb11.first}}\)
\[ \delta \;=\; \mathbb{E}[Y \mid Z{=}1] - \mathbb{E}[Y \mid Z{=}0] \] reduced form: how much the nudge moved sales, through whatever channel
\[ \hat\beta_{\text{IV}} \;=\; \frac{\delta}{\pi} \;=\; \frac{\text{nudge} \to \text{sales}}{\text{nudge} \to \text{exposure}} \] the sales the nudge earned, divided by the exposure it bought: €{{nb11.iv_est}}
Why the division works
The nudge raised sales only by raising exposure (that is exclusion). So dividing the sales effect by the exposure effect strips out the scale of the nudge and leaves the price of one exposure. No sampler, no priors: the assumptions did the work.
The two-stage least-squares form, and why it equals the ratio
\[ \hat X = \hat\Pr(X{=}1 \mid Z), \qquad \hat\beta_{\text{IV}} = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(\hat X, Y)}{\operatorname{Var}(\hat X)} \]

Regress \(X\) on \(Z\) to get fitted \(\hat X\); regress \(Y\) on \(\hat X\). With one binary instrument and no controls, that two-stage procedure equals \(\delta/\pi\) exactly. With covariates it generalises; the ratio intuition survives.

Act II · The method · the load-bearing assumption

Break exclusion on purpose, and watch the estimate move

▶ LIVE a side-channel from the nudge to sales
Slide \(d\) up: even a tiny direct effect of the nudge on sales throws the estimate off, because the bias is \(d/\pi\) and \(\pi\) is small.

If the queue bump also changed sales any other way (a different price shown, a loading-speed change), exclusion breaks. The reduced form now carries that side-channel too, and the ratio inherits it:

\[ \hat\beta_{\text{IV}} \;=\; \frac{\delta + d}{\pi} \;=\; \beta + \frac{d}{\pi} \] bias \(= d/\pi\): divided by a small first stage, a small \(d\) becomes a large error
No test will catch it
Exclusion is an assumption about a path that does not exist, so there is nothing in the data to check. You defend it by design: pick an instrument so content-free that a side-channel is implausible, then price how big one would need to be to flip your decision.
Act II · The method · hands on

Find the effect that reproduces the reduced form

▶ LIVE your candidate effect vs the data
A candidate effect \(\hat\beta\) predicts a reduced form of \(\hat\beta \times \pi\). Slide until your predicted bar matches the sales the nudge actually produced. The match is the Wald estimate.

The instrument gives you two facts: the nudge moved exposure by \(\pi\), and moved sales by \(\delta\). Any claimed effect \(\hat\beta\) predicts a reduced form of \(\hat\beta \times \pi\).

\[ \hat\beta \times \pi \;\stackrel{!}{=}\; \delta \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \hat\beta \;=\; \frac{\delta}{\pi} \] the effect that reproduces the data is the ratio: no other value fits
This is all 2SLS is
Not a black box. It is the single value of the per-exposure effect that makes the nudge's sales bump add up. You just found it by hand.

Act III · Is it real, and how big

The usual test is confident, and confidently wrong

two intervals, one truth
The naive interval is five times tighter and misses the truth completely. The instrument's interval is wide and contains it. Precision is not validity.

Run the textbook test on the naive gap and it screams significance: €{{nb11.naive}} with a standard error of €{{nb11.naive_se}}, so a 90% interval of [{{nb11.naive_lo}}, {{nb11.naive_hi}}]. A p-value near zero.

Every word of that is arithmetically right and the interval does not contain the truth. A test answers "could this be noise?" It never asks "am I measuring the right thing?" Bias is not noise, and no amount of data shrinks it.

More data makes it worse, not better
As \(n\) grows the naive interval tightens around the wrong number. Precision converges on the bias. This is why "we have millions of rows" is not an answer to a confounding objection.
The instrument's price
2SLS pays for validity with width: SE €{{nb11.iv_se}} against the naive €{{nb11.naive_se}}. That is the trade the whole lecture makes: a wide honest number beats a tight wrong one.

Before you trust the ratio

Act III · Is it real, and how big · the check fires

A weak instrument is worse than none

▶ LIVE what the estimator does as the first stage dies
Each point is the median Wald across repeats at that first-stage \(F\); the band is the 5th to 95th percentile. Drag \(\gamma\) down and watch both the spread explode and the centre drift back up toward OLS.

The method divides by \(\pi\). As the instrument weakens, the denominator approaches zero and the ratio becomes unstable in two ways:

  • the interval balloons, which is honest and expected;
  • the centre creeps back toward the naive OLS number, which is not expected. A near-zero denominator reintroduces exactly the bias the instrument was hired to remove.
Why this is the dangerous failure
A weak instrument does not announce itself as noise. It produces a plausible number with a plausible interval that is quietly biased toward the thing you were trying to escape. Report the first-stage \(F\) or your causal claim is undefended.
The check that fires
\(F\) below 10 is the alarm. When it sounds you do not "interpret with caution": you either walk away, or you use an interval that stays valid at any strength. That is the next slide.
Act III · Is it real, and how big · the interval

Build the interval by asking every candidate effect

▶ LIVE Anderson and Rubin: test each \(\beta_0\), keep the survivors
The green band is the set of candidate effects the data cannot reject: the Anderson and Rubin confidence set, [{{nb11.ar_lo}}, {{nb11.ar_hi}}]. It needs no estimate of \(\beta\) at all, so it stays valid even when the instrument is weak.

Pick a candidate effect \(\beta_0\). If it were true, subtracting it should leave sales with no trace of the lottery:

\[ Y - \beta_0 X \;\perp\; Z \qquad \text{if } \beta_0 = \beta \] strip the true effect and the coin should predict nothing that remains
\[ \mathrm{AR}(\beta_0) \;=\; \left( \frac{\delta - \beta_0\,\pi}{\mathrm{se}} \right)^{\!2} \;\le\; 2.71 \] test that the coin's leftover effect is zero; keep every \(\beta_0\) that survives at 90%
Why bother
An interval built as "estimate ± 1.645 se" trusts the estimate. AR never forms a ratio, so it cannot be destabilised by a small \(\pi\). Here the instrument is strong and AR [{{nb11.ar_lo}}, {{nb11.ar_hi}}] nearly matches 2SLS [{{nb11.iv_lo}}, {{nb11.iv_hi}}]. When \(F\) is small, they part company and AR is the one to believe.

Act IV · The decision, in euros · a P&L error, not a statistics error

Know which line of the P&L your β lives on

▶ LIVE from effect to net, one exposure
This chapter's \(\beta\) is already value per exposure, so the base case is 100% and the net is €{{nb11.net}}. Slide the margin down to see where a revenue-denominated \(\beta\) would flip the same campaign into a loser.

The model hands you an effect on sales. The bid spends cash. If your \(\beta\) is revenue and your gross margin is \(m\), the exposure only pays when

\[ m \cdot \beta \;>\; c \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad m \;>\; c/\beta \] break-even margin: below it, a real, significant, well-identified effect still loses money
This is a reading error, not a statistics problem
No estimator can save you here. A campaign can be statistically real and commercially worthless, and the mistake is not in the model: it is in reading a revenue number as if it were profit. Ask which line of the P&L the outcome variable was measured on before you compare it to a price.

What is still missing
Two things a confidence interval cannot give: the probability that an exposure pays at a given price, and the most we can defend paying. For those we need a distribution over \(\beta\), not a range. That is the bridge.
Bridge · what the classical report cannot say

The same estimate and interval, three different decisions

all three report "€{{nb11.iv_est}}, se €{{nb11.iv_se}}"
Three belief shapes with the same mean and the same standard error. A classical report cannot tell them apart, yet they disagree about how often a €18 bid pays.

The classical arm hands you two numbers: a point and a spread. Those two numbers do not pin down a shape, and the decision lives in the shape's tail.

Ask "at a price of €18, what is the chance this pays?" and the three worlds answer differently, while filing identical reports.

The gap, precisely
A confidence interval is a statement about the procedure: over many repeated datasets, 90% of intervals built this way cover the truth. It is not a statement about this effect. So it cannot be integrated, and "the probability the exposure pays" has no home in it.
What fills the gap
A posterior: a distribution over \(\beta\) given the data we actually have. Once you have that object, the decision questions become integrals. That is the only thing the Bayesian arm is here to add. It does not get a vote on whether to buy: we already banked that.
Act V · Adding a probability · the ten minute primer

Prior times likelihood, renormalised

▶ LIVE update a belief about \(\beta\)
Widen the prior and the posterior collapses onto the data. Tighten it onto a wrong value and you can drag the answer: that is the honest risk of a prior, and why we price it rather than hide it.

Here \(\theta = \beta\): the euros of sales one exposure causes. Three objects, one rule:

\[ \underbrace{p(\beta \mid \text{data})}_{\text{posterior}} \;\propto\; \underbrace{p(\text{data} \mid \beta)}_{\text{likelihood}} \times \underbrace{p(\beta)}_{\text{prior}} \] what you believed, reweighted by how well each \(\beta\) explains what you saw
  • Prior: belief before the data. Ours is deliberately vague.
  • Likelihood: the instrument's evidence, centred near €{{nb11.iv_est}}.
  • Posterior: the answer, a full distribution rather than a range.
The eye-calibration
At our sample size the likelihood is sharp and the prior is flat, so the posterior sits almost exactly on the data. Drag the prior sd to 1 and watch the prior win: that is what "the prior did the work" looks like, and it is a thing you can see rather than a thing you must trust.
Act V · Adding a probability · the model

The library's default models a coin flip with a bell curve

The standard Bayesian IV ships as one joint Gaussian over two equations: exposure and sales, correlated through \(\rho\). Written out, its first row says exposure is normally distributed. Exposure is 0 or 1. That row cannot be true.

\[ \begin{pmatrix} X \\ Y \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \begin{pmatrix} \alpha_0 + \gamma Z \\ \mu + \beta X \end{pmatrix},\; \Sigma(\rho) \right) \] the first row is a linear probability model for a binary treatment: convenient, and wrong

It costs real money: the joint-Gaussian fit lands at €{{nb11.gauss_mean}}, off by €{{nb11.gauss_err}}, while 2SLS with no priors at all sits on the truth.

The repair
Give the first stage a link that knows the treatment is binary: a probit. Same instrument, same assumptions, same estimand. The repaired posterior mean is €{{nb11.bayes_mean}} against a truth of €15, and it agrees with 2SLS to a rounding error.
with pm.Model() as probit_iv:
    # first stage: a BINARY treatment needs a binary link
    a0  = pm.Normal("a0", 0, 2)
    gam = pm.Normal("gam", 0, 2)
    eta = a0 + gam * Z                      # latent exposure index
    pm.Bernoulli("X", p=pm.math.invprobit(eta), observed=X)

    # the endogeneity, named and estimated
    rho  = pm.Uniform("rho", -1, 1)
    beta = pm.Normal("beta", 0, 20)     # the euros we are buying
    sig  = pm.HalfNormal("sig", 10)

    # sales, shifted by the part of exposure the intent explains
    mu_y = mu0 + beta * X + rho * sig * resid(eta, X)
    pm.Normal("Y", mu_y, sig * pm.math.sqrt(1 - rho**2), observed=Y)
The one genuinely new object
\(\rho\) is the correlation between the intent that drives exposure and the intent that drives sales. 2SLS cannot report it. Here it is {{nb11.rho}}, 90% credible [{{nb11.rho_lo}}, {{nb11.rho_hi}}], with \(P(\rho > 0) = {{nb11.rho_p_pos}}\): the self-selection, measured rather than alleged.

Act V · Adding a probability · the decision integral

The question a posterior can answer and an interval cannot

▶ LIVE the posterior, cut at the price
Green mass is where the exposure pays. The bid cap is the price at which that mass falls to 90%: the posterior's 10th percentile, €{{nb11.cap}}.
\[ \Pr(\beta > c \mid \text{data}) \;=\; \int_{c}^{\infty} p(\beta \mid \text{data})\, d\beta \] the share of the posterior above the price: a number a manager can act on
  • At €{{nb11.cost}}: the exposure pays with probability 1.00. Every defensible \(\beta\) clears the price.

Why the cap is not the estimate
Bidding your point estimate (€{{nb11.iv_est}}) means being wrong half the time by construction. The cap builds the margin for error into the price. Headroom: €{{nb11.headroom}} between the cap and today's €{{nb11.cost}} rate.

At the €{{nb11.cost}} price the exposure pays with probability 1.00 and the headroom is €{{nb11.headroom}}. Analytics offers to run a larger lottery for a few weeks, sharpening \(\beta\). Here \(k\) is the extra cost of that study, per exposure. What is that evidence worth?
C. Evidence is only worth what it changes. At €{{nb11.cost}} every value of \(\beta\) the posterior still entertains says "buy", so no sharpening can flip the action: the value of information is €{{nb11.voi}}, whatever \(k\) is. Move the price to the coin flip (€{{nb11.coinflip}}), where the decision is genuinely balanced, and the same study is suddenly worth €{{nb11.voi_coin}} per exposure. Buy evidence when the decision is close, not when the interval is wide.
Act V · Adding a probability · what more certainty is worth

Price the study before you run it

\[ \mathrm{VOI} \;=\; \mathbb{E}\big[\text{payoff with perfect information}\big] \;-\; \text{payoff of acting now} \] what you would pay to know \(\beta\) exactly, before committing
\[ \mathrm{VOI}(c) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\big[\max(\beta - c,\, 0)\big] \;-\; \max\big(\mathbb{E}[\beta] - c,\, 0\big) \] perfect information only helps where the sign of \(\beta - c\) is still in doubt
  • At the €{{nb11.cost}} plan: VOI = €{{nb11.voi}}. The posterior never crosses the price, so knowledge buys nothing.
  • At the coin flip €{{nb11.coinflip}}: VOI = €{{nb11.voi_coin}}. Now both actions are live and information has a price.
The counter-intuitive rule
A wide interval is not a reason to study more. A close decision is. Our interval is wide (€{{nb11.iv_lo}} to €{{nb11.iv_hi}}) and the decision is not close, so the correct next step is to spend the money on exposures, not on measurement.
Where it would bite
If the DSP raised the rate toward €{{nb11.cap}}, the headroom would vanish, VOI would climb, and the same study you just declined becomes the best money on the table. Re-price the study whenever the rate card moves.

"Should we measure more?" is not a statistics question either. It is a comparison between the value of information and its cost.

The probit does not merely underperform: it reproduces the dashboard's number ({{nb11b.probit_est}} against the naive {{nb11b.naive}}), and its \(\rho\) collapses to {{nb11b.probit_rho}}, reporting no endogeneity in a dataset built out of endogeneity.

Why, and why it is not embarrassing
With a binary instrument and a binary treatment the first stage is saturated: two groups, two proportions. A linear model fits those two numbers exactly. The probit adds a curved link where there is no curve to fit, and buys nothing while risking everything. The simulated twin rewarded the probit because its first stage had a continuous latent index. Criteo's does not.
The most important output a method has
"This does not apply here." A model that transfers because it is more sophisticated is a story; a model that transfers because its assumptions still hold is a method. We caught this only because we had a referee. Without the anchor we would have shipped the wrong number and called it an upgrade.

The verdict above does not move: 2SLS and the shipped linear posterior both hit the anchor. What moved is our confidence in one specific refinement, and it moved because we checked.

Takeaways · what transfers to your next problem

Seven things worth keeping

1 · Endogenous treatment
When the thing you want to price chose itself, no amount of controls saves you. Ask what assigned the treatment before you ask what it did.
2 · Find the coin
An instrument is a lever that moves the treatment, is assigned by something blind to intent, and touches the outcome no other way. Lotteries, queues, capacity limits, cost shocks.
3 · Reduced form over first stage
That is the whole estimator. If you cannot explain your causal number as a ratio of two simple differences, you do not yet understand it.
4 · Report the F
A weak instrument is worse than none: precisely wrong, drifting back to the bias you fled. Below F = 10, walk away or use Anderson and Rubin.
5 · Precision is not validity
The naive interval was five times tighter and missed the truth. More rows shrink noise, never bias.
6 · Classical decides, Bayes prices
The action was settled by the interval. The posterior added P(pays), the cap, and the VOI. Wanting a probability is not a reason to change paradigm mid-argument.
7 · Buy evidence only when the decision is close
VOI was €{{nb11.voi}} at our price and €{{nb11.voi_coin}} at the coin flip. A wide interval is not a reason to measure more.
And the one that outranks them all
Your instrument's exclusion is untestable. It is defended by the design you chose, not by any output you can print. If the honest answer is "this instrument might touch sales another way", the honest output is "I cannot measure this", and that is a result, not a failure.
Endnote · advanced note

A last trap, for the people who will run this

✋ Poll
A colleague brings you an IV study: the estimate is €18.2, the 90% interval is a tight [17.9, 18.5], and the first-stage \(F\) is 3. How much do you trust the interval?
B. A tight interval around a weak-instrument estimate is the most dangerous artefact in this lecture. The usual "estimate ± 1.645 se" formula assumes the ratio is well behaved; with \(F = 3\) it is not, and the interval's stated coverage is fiction. You cannot repair it by widening it by taste (C). You report an Anderson and Rubin set, which stays valid at any strength and will typically be enormous, or you say you cannot measure it. Precision is not validity: it was true in Act III and it is still true here.
Endnote · advanced note

The joint Gaussian missed. Which part was guilty?

The joint-Gaussian fit landed €{{nb11.gauss_err}} from the truth. "Bayes was biased" is a lazy verdict. The chapter puts three suspects in the room and clears two of them:

SuspectVerdict
the likelihood (bell curve on a coin flip)contributes
the prior on the endogeneity \(\rho\)innocent
the prior on the error scalesguilty

Flatten the \(\rho\) prior and the bias barely moves: it was never the endogeneity prior doing the damage, which is the thing everyone blames first.

Why this matters for how you work
The instinct on a biased Bayesian fit is to argue about the prior on the parameter of interest. Here that instinct is wrong twice over: the guilty prior was on a nuisance scale, and the likelihood was misspecified. Debug the model, not the paradigm.
The endnote's job
The verdict above does not depend on this. The action (buy at €{{nb11.cost}}), the cap (€{{nb11.cap}}) and the real-data check all come from the classical arm and the shipped model. This slide exists so that the people who will re-run it know where the bodies are.

Endnote · advanced note

Three tools this case did not need

Anderson and Rubin
We built the set live in Act III and it nearly matched 2SLS, because \(F = {{nb11.f_stat}}\). Its value is at small \(F\), where it is the only interval that still means anything. Keep it in the drawer for the day the first stage is thin.
Abadie's κ
Compliers are invisible: you never learn who they are. Abadie's κ re-weights the sample so you can profile them, and check whether the LATE speaks for a population your bid can actually reach. Appendix 13.A.14.
Lee bounds
If the nudge also changed who reports an outcome, the sample is selected and even IV is compromised. Lee bounds bracket the effect without assuming the attrition away. Appendix 13.A.15.
Why they are endnotes and not the argument
Each answers a question this case does not raise: the instrument is strong, the compliers are the bid's target by construction, and nothing attrits. Wheeling them out anyway would be method theatre. Reach for them when the specific failure appears, and not before.
The transferable habit
Every one of these is a response to a named threat. Learn the threats first: weak first stage, unknown compliers, selective reporting. The tools are easy to look up once you can name what went wrong.
Close

The whole argument, in one breath

The platform showed us a gap of €{{nb11.naive}} and asked us to bid on it. The gap was mostly the platform's own targeting: it had picked buyers, then billed us for their intent.

We could not randomise exposure, so we found a coin the platform was already flipping. It moved exposure by {{nb11.first}} and moved sales by a matching amount. Dividing the two gave €{{nb11.iv_est}}: what an exposure is worth to the users a bid can actually move.

We checked the coin was strong (F = {{nb11.f_stat}}), admitted the one assumption no test can reach, and banked the decision classically: at €{{nb11.cost}}, buy. Then the posterior priced it: cap €{{nb11.cap}}, headroom €{{nb11.headroom}}, and more evidence worth €{{nb11.voi}}. On {{nb11b.n_full_m}}M real rows the same method landed on the design anchor, and the one refinement that did not transfer told us so.

If you keep one thing

Before you price anything, ask what assigned the treatment. If the answer is "the thing that also drives the outcome", the number on your dashboard is not the number to bid on, and you need a coin.
