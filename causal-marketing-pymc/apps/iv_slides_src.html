Part 1 · The problem

A retailer, an ad platform, and one number

Every concept in this lecture is built on one business question. Here it is, in full.

The setup, in four facts:

  • An online retailer pays an ad platform to show its display ads. One exposure means one user actually saw an ad.
  • Each exposure costs €{{nb11.cost}}.
  • The platform's dashboard shows that users who saw an ad went on to spend €{{nb11.naive}} more than users who did not.
  • The platform's pitch: "an exposure is worth €{{nb11.naive}} and costs €{{nb11.cost}}. Raise the budget."

The decision: keep paying €{{nb11.cost}} per exposure, pay more, or stop; it turns on one number, how many euros of sales one exposure causes.

The word doing the work: cause
"Exposed users spend €{{nb11.naive}} more" and "an exposure causes €{{nb11.naive}} of sales" are the same claim only if the two groups were comparable to begin with.
  • A difference: the dashboard number is arithmetic about two groups, and the arithmetic is correct.
  • The targeting: the platform chose who saw the ads, and it is paid to choose people who already look ready to buy.
The catch
The exposed group was hand-picked to spend more, ad or no ad: the two groups were not comparable.

Part 1 · The problem

What the dashboard actually computed

The dashboard's number is the naive comparison: the average sales of the exposed, minus the average sales of the unexposed.

\[ \widehat{\Delta}_{\text{naive}} \;=\; \bar{Y}_{\text{exposed}} \;-\; \bar{Y}_{\text{unexposed}} \;=\; \text{€}{{nb11.naive}} \] a difference of two averages: correct arithmetic, and still possibly a wrong answer about the ad
  • Tick the box: the exposed users sit at higher buying intent, so they would have spent more with no ad at all.
This is selection
The groups differed before the ad did anything.

Part 1 · The problem · the first definition

The problem has a name: confounding

Definition · confounder
A confounder is a variable that influences both the treatment and the outcome.
  • Here it is intent \(U\): it raises the chance the platform shows the ad (the targeting) and raises sales on its own (ready buyers buy, ad or not).
  • What the naive gap adds: the ad's effect plus the pre-existing difference between picked and skipped, inseparably.
  • The closed door: the textbook fix is to compare users of equal intent, but intent is a feeling in a shopper's head, in no log file.
the situation, as a diagram
\(U\) points at both \(X\) and \(Y\): the fork is the confound, and the naive gap measures arrow and fork together.
Another way to say it
The naive comparison substitutes the unexposed users' sales for the exposed users' missing no-ad sales, and selection makes that substitute too low.
Part 1 · The problem · a world we can grade

To study the problem, we build a world where we know the answer

Four equations generate everything you saw. We planted the truth: one exposure causes exactly €{{nb11.true}} of sales.
\[ U \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1), \qquad Z \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(0.5) \] \(U\) = buying intent, unobserved · \(Z\) = the lottery from the cast list, a genuine coin flip (its moment comes in Part 2)
\[ \Pr(X{=}1) \;=\; \sigma\!\left(\alpha_0 + \gamma\,Z + \lambda\,U\right) \] who sees an ad: partly the coin \(\gamma Z\), partly intent \(\lambda U\). The \(\lambda U\) part is the targeting.
\[ Y \;=\; \mu + \underbrace{\beta}_{\text{€15, planted}} X \;+\; \underbrace{\kappa\,U}_{\text{the confound}} \;+\; \varepsilon \] sales: the ad effect \(\beta\) we want, plus intent \(\kappa U\) flowing straight into sales
Why simulate at all
We planted \(\beta =\) €{{nb11.true}}, so every method in this lecture can be graded; Part 6 repeats the exam on real data.
  • Dial \(\kappa\): how strongly intent leaks into sales; turn it up and the confounding grows.
  • Dial \(\gamma\): how strongly the coin moves exposure; it becomes important in Part 2, and you drive both on the next slide.

One shared hidden cause, \(U\) in both equations, is the entire problem.

Part 1 · The problem · the disease, stated exactly

One inequality is the whole problem

Collect everything the sales equation leaves out into a single error term \(v\):

\[ Y \;=\; \mu + \beta\,X + v, \qquad v \;=\; \kappa\,U + \varepsilon \] \(v\) is "every other reason this customer spent money". Intent sits inside it, with weight \(\kappa\).
  • Exposure is not innocent of it: the auction targets intent (the \(\lambda U\) term), so \(X\) and \(U\) move together.
\[ \operatorname{Cov}(X,\, v) \;=\; \kappa\,\operatorname{Cov}(X,\, U) \;>\; 0 \] positive because both leaks are positive: targeting pulls intent into exposure, and ready buyers buy. The noise \(\varepsilon\) contributes nothing, it is independent of everything.
Definition · endogeneity
A regressor correlated with the error term of its own equation is endogenous: the variable whose price we want is correlated with the things we left out.
  • Here: exposure is endogenous, \(\operatorname{Cov}(X, v) > 0\), and everything else in the lecture is a response to that inequality.
  • Read it exactly: the correlation is with the entire error \(v\), not with some listable set of forgotten controls.
The sharp edge
The variable driving the correlation was never written to disk; hold that thought for two slides.
Part 1 · The problem · the bias, computed

How wrong is the dashboard? There is a formula

\[ \widehat{\Delta}_{\text{naive}} \;\longrightarrow\; \beta \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, v)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)}}_{\text{omitted-variable bias}} \;=\; \beta \;+\; \kappa\,\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, U)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)} \] the standard result: the naive slope converges to the truth plus the disease, scaled by exposure's variance. Both factors of the bias are positive, so the dashboard can only overstate.
  • Predicted vs delivered: the formula predicts a naive gap near €{{nb11.ols_predicted}} (bias {{nb11.ovb}}); the data delivered €{{nb11.naive}} against a truth of €{{nb11.true}}.
  • What makes it big: strong leakage of intent into sales (\(\kappa\)) and strong targeting (\(\operatorname{Cov}(X,U)\)); sample size is absent, so more rows shrink noise, not bias.
  • The tight lie: the dashboard's band, €{{nb11.naive_lo}} to €{{nb11.naive_hi}}, is the tightest in this lecture and misses €{{nb11.true}} completely.
What the dashboard measures
Not the ad's power to create buyers but the platform's skill at finding them, reported as one number.

Part 1 · The problem · closing the obvious exit

No amount of controlling fixes this

  • The first instinct: adjust; add controls, match, weight by a propensity score, or hand the features to an ML model.
  • One shared engine: compare users equal on the recorded features \(W\) and trust that within those groups exposure is as good as random.

Formally, every one of them needs:

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(X,\, v \mid W) \;=\; 0 \qquad \text{for some } W \text{ that was actually recorded} \] "once the recorded features are held fixed, nothing hidden drives both exposure and sales"
Why the engine stalls here
You cannot adjust for what you did not record.
  • The mechanics: the correlation is generated by \(\kappa U\), and \(U\) is in no \(W\) you can construct.
  • Partial credit only: conditioning on recorded features removes the confounding that runs through them and leaves the rest untouched.
  • Graded in Part 3: a regression armed with every recorded control lands within rounding of the naive €{{nb11.naive}}.
Where this leaves us
The problem needs a genuinely different idea, not a better estimator of the same family; that idea is Part 2.

Part 2 · The idea · what we wish we could do

Randomization would solve this. We do not control the coin.

  • The wish: show the ad to a random half ourselves; randomness ignores intent, and the bias formula from Part 1 reads zero.
\[ X \text{ randomized} \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \operatorname{Cov}(X,\, v) = 0 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; \widehat{\Delta}_{\text{naive}} \longrightarrow \beta \] randomization kills the disease at its definition: with \(\operatorname{Cov}(X,v)=0\), the naive comparison becomes the right comparison
  • The wall: the platform's auction decides exposure in milliseconds, using exactly the intent signals that cause the problem.
The idea that saves us
If we cannot inject randomness into exposure, find the randomness the system already contains and use only that part.
  • Where it hides: a coin flip, a lottery, an arbitrary rule that moved exposure for reasons unrelated to intent.
The plan for Part 2
1. Give that random lever a name: an instrument.
2. State exactly the four conditions it must satisfy.
3. Check which of the four the data can verify, and which we must argue for.
Part 2 · The idea · the second definition

An instrument is borrowed randomness

Definition · instrument
An instrument \(Z\) moves the treatment, is as good as random with respect to the hidden confounder, and affects the outcome only through the treatment; a fourth condition, about people rather than paths, completes the set.
1 · Relevance
\(Z\) actually moves exposure \(X\). testable measured by the first stage, next slides.
2 · Exogeneity
\(Z\) is unrelated to intent \(U\). by design if \(Z\) is a genuine coin flip.
3 · Exclusion
\(Z\) touches sales only via exposure. untestable argued, never checked.
4 · Monotonicity
the coin never blocks an exposure it would otherwise have caused. untestable what it buys comes in Part 4.
  • What they buy: 1 and 2 buy a clean experiment on the coin; 3 converts it into a statement about the ad; 4 says whose effect you measured.
An instrument is the opposite of a control
You do not hold \(Z\) fixed; you use the variation it created.
the fork, and the way around it
Top: the confounded fork from Part 1. Bottom: the instrument \(Z\) pushes on \(X\) from outside. No arrow from \(U\) to \(Z\) (condition 2), no arrow from \(Z\) straight to \(Y\) (condition 3).
Part 2 · The idea · recognising one

Our instrument, and three famous ones

  • Ours, by construction: a serving-priority lottery bumps a random half of users in the ad auction, changing nothing except the odds of exposure.
  • Relevance: a queue bump means more ads served.
  • Exogeneity: it is a coin, so it cannot be correlated with intent.
  • Exclusion: a queue position shows the user nothing, so the only route to sales runs through the ad; argued from the design.

The skill is scouting: in your company the instrument is a rollout order, a capacity limit, an upstream A/B test, a rounding rule.

The same pattern built several Nobel-cited results:

  • The shared shape: something arbitrary nudged a treatment nobody randomized; school-entry rules tie birth quarter to schooling, a televised lottery decided who was drafted.
The genius is never the estimator
It is spotting the coin the world already flipped.
Part 2 · The idea · condition 1, measured

Did the coin actually move exposure? Look.

Condition 1 is a claim about the data, so the data can answer it: compare the exposure rate in the two coin groups:

\[ \pi \;=\; \Pr(X{=}1 \mid Z{=}1) \;-\; \Pr(X{=}1 \mid Z{=}0) \;=\; {{nb11.first}} \] the first stage: exposure climbs from {{nb11.fs_lo}}% among coin losers to {{nb11.fs_hi}}% among winners, {{nb11.fs_shift}} points per flip

The same number, written as the regression we will build on:

\[ X \;=\; b_0 + \pi\,Z + u \] \(b_0\) = exposure rate among coin losers · \(u\) = every other reason for exposure, intent included
  • Unconfounded, full stop: \(Z\) is random, so \(\hat\pi\) is a causal effect of the coin; keep this line, we substitute into it shortly.
  • How surely: the first-stage \(F\) asks how far \(\pi\) is from zero in units of its own noise; here \(F = {{nb11.f_stat}}\), far above the folklore bar of 10.
exposure rate, by the coin
The step between the bars is the first stage \(\pi\): the slice of exposure that randomness, not targeting, created.
Part 2 · The idea · conditions 2 and 3

The coin must be blind, and it must be silent

  • Blind (exogeneity): the coin must not favour high-intent users; ours is a genuine randomizer, so condition 2 holds by design.
  • Silent (exclusion): the coin must not touch sales through any channel except the ad; a side-channel \(d\) (page speed, a shown price) contaminates everything:
\[ \hat\beta_{\text{IV}} \;=\; \beta + \frac{d}{\pi} \] a side-channel \(d\) gets divided by the small first stage \(\pi\): a tiny leak becomes a large error
No test will catch it
Exclusion is a claim about a path that should not exist, so no output can verify it; you defend it by design.
  • Our defence: a queue bump is content-free, sells nothing, and the user never learns it happened.
  • The counterexample: an email with a discount code would move spending on its own, exclusion would be false, and everything after this slide would collapse.
▶ LIVE break the silence on purpose
Slide \(d\) up: even a small direct effect of the coin on sales pulls the estimate off target, because the damage is \(d/\pi\) and \(\pi\) is only {{nb11.first}}.
Part 2 · The idea

Before we build the estimator

✋ Poll
Four conditions make an instrument: relevance (\(Z\) moves \(X\)), exogeneity (\(Z\) is unrelated to \(U\)), exclusion (\(Z\) reaches \(Y\) only through \(X\)), monotonicity (the coin never blocks an exposure it would have caused). Which of them can the data verify?
B. Relevance is a claim about observable data, so we measure it: \(F = {{nb11.f_stat}}\). Exogeneity cannot be tested against an unobserved \(U\), but a genuine randomizer guarantees it by design. Exclusion is untestable in principle: \(Z\) is supposed to predict \(Y\) (through \(X\)), so option C's test detects nothing. Monotonicity is argued from the mechanism: a priority bump can only raise the odds of exposure. The honest scorecard for any IV study is: one number, one design guarantee, two arguments. Keep that in mind when you read one.
Part 3 · The estimator · the second ingredient

Did the coin move sales? Look again.

Now the same comparison for the outcome: average sales in the two coin groups, not the two exposure groups:

\[ \delta \;=\; \mathbb{E}[Y \mid Z{=}1] \;-\; \mathbb{E}[Y \mid Z{=}0] \;=\; \text{€}{{nb11.reduced}} \] the reduced form (policy name: intention-to-treat): extra euros of sales per coin flip, causal because the coin is random
  • Why it is worth anything: substitute the first-stage line into the sales equation and watch what multiplies \(Z\):
\[ Y \;=\; \mu + \beta\,(b_0 + \pi Z + u) + v \;=\; (\mu + \beta b_0) \;+\; \underbrace{\beta\,\pi}_{\;=\;\delta\text{'s slope}}\,Z \;+\; (\beta u + v) \] the coin's slope on sales is exactly \(\beta \times \pi\): how much the coin moves exposure, times how much exposure moves sales
Both numbers are correct; neither answers the question
\(\pi\) is exposures per flip and \(\delta\) is euros per flip, but the client asked for euros per exposure: since \(\delta = \beta\pi\), one step is left.
average sales, by the coin
The step is small but clean: a random coin cannot favour ready buyers, so selection cannot explain it.
Part 3 · The estimator · the whole method in one division

Euros per flip, divided by exposures per flip

Track the units. One coin flip buys, on average:

  • {{nb11.first}} extra exposures per user (the first stage \(\pi\)), and
  • €{{nb11.reduced}} extra sales per user (the reduced form \(\delta\)).

The last slide proved \(\delta = \beta\,\pi\); we measured the product \(\delta\) and the factor \(\pi\), so solving for \(\beta\) is one division:

\[ \hat\beta_{\text{IV}} \;=\; \frac{\delta}{\pi} \;=\; \frac{ {{nb11.reduced}} }{ {{nb11.first}} } \;=\; \text{€}{{nb11.wald}} \] the Wald estimate. Check the units, they are the intuition: euros per flip over exposures per flip leaves euros per exposure.
What just happened
We priced the ad using only the random slice of exposure: the dashboard said €{{nb11.naive}}, the coin says €{{nb11.wald}}, against a planted truth of €{{nb11.true}}.
  • Why intent never enters: it cannot correlate with a coin, so it cancels out of numerator and denominator alike.
  • Three names, one idea: the instrumental-variables estimate; the Wald estimator for a binary coin; 2SLS in software, printing €{{nb11.iv_est}} here to a rounding error.

The method never needed a model of intent, controls, or machine learning: two averages and a division.

Part 3 · The estimator · hands on

Convince yourself: only one effect size fits the data

▶ LIVE your candidate effect vs the data
A candidate effect \(\hat\beta\) predicts a reduced form of \(\hat\beta \times \pi\). Slide until the predicted bar matches the sales the coin actually produced. The value that fits is the Wald estimate.
  • Forward logic: if the effect were \(\hat\beta\), the coin's \(\pi\) exposures per user should have created \(\hat\beta \times \pi\) euros per user.
  • But we measured it: the coin actually created \(\delta\) euros per user.
\[ \hat\beta \times \pi \;\stackrel{!}{=}\; \delta \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \hat\beta \;=\; \frac{\delta}{\pi} \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(Y, Z)}{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Z)} \] read per condition: numerator trustworthy (\(Z\) random), denominator nonzero (\(Z\) relevant), ratio equals \(\beta\) (exclusion forces \(\operatorname{Cov}(v, Z) = 0\))
Not a black box
Every IV estimate is the effect size that makes the instrument's sales bump add up; if you cannot state yours as a ratio of two simple differences, you do not yet understand it.
Part 3 · The estimator · what the software runs

Two-stage least squares, stage by stage

Software packages compute the same number as two regressions, which is where the name comes from:

\[ \textbf{Stage 1:}\;\; \hat{X} = \hat b_0 + \hat\pi\,Z \] keep the predicted exposure: a function of the coin alone, uncorrelated with intent by construction
\[ \textbf{Stage 2:}\;\; Y = \mu + \beta\,\hat{X} + e \] regress sales on the purified exposure; the \(U\)-contaminated part of \(X\) never enters
  • Exactly, not approximately: with one instrument for one endogenous regressor (the just-identified case) this returns \(\delta/\pi\) to floating point; the regression form is more convenient, not smarter.
Three things the regression form buys
1. Standard errors (the ± on an estimate; Part 4 treats them properly), computed correctly in one pass.
2. Covariates: add observed controls to both stages to tighten the interval.
3. Several instruments at once, when you have more than one coin.
One warning that saves careers
If you run the two stages by hand, do not read the second stage's printed standard error.
  • Why: it treats \(\hat X\) as data and charges \(\hat\beta\) for first-stage prediction error that is not part of its sampling noise.
  • Measured, against folklore: the hand-rolled error is too wide, not too narrow; {{nb11.se_hand}} against a correct {{nb11.se_correct}}, a factor of {{nb11.se_hand_ratio}}.
  • The fix: a proper IV routine (linearmodels.IV2SLS, ivreg) gets it right in one pass.

Mental model: 2SLS = "filter exposure through the coin, then price the filtered part."

Part 4 · When it breaks · precision vs validity

The usual significance test is confident, and confidently wrong

two intervals, one truth
The naive interval is five times tighter, and misses the truth entirely. The IV interval is wide, and contains it. Precision is not validity.
  • Standard error (se): how much an estimate would wobble across repeat samples.
  • 90% confidence interval: estimate ± 1.645 se, the range of effects the data cannot rule out.
  • The naive gap screams significance: €{{nb11.naive}} with se €{{nb11.naive_se}}, interval [{{nb11.naive_lo}}, {{nb11.naive_hi}}], p-value near zero, truth outside.
  • The wrong question: a significance test asks "could this be noise?", never "am I measuring the right thing?"; bias is not noise.
  • More data makes it worse: as \(n\) grows the naive interval tightens around the wrong number, so "we have millions of rows" answers nothing.
The trade IV makes
IV pays for validity with width (se €{{nb11.iv_se}} against €{{nb11.naive_se}}), and a wide honest number beats a tight wrong one.
Part 4 · When it breaks

Before you trust the ratio

✋ Poll
Our coin moves exposure from {{nb11.fs_lo}}% to {{nb11.fs_hi}}%, a first stage of {{nb11.first}}, and the first-stage \(F\) is {{nb11.f_stat}}. Is that strong enough to trust \(\delta/\pi\)?
A. What matters is not whether \(\pi\) is large but whether it is precisely measured: we are about to divide by it. \(F = {{nb11.f_stat}}\) says the first stage is pinned down far from zero, so the division is safe. The rule of thumb wants \(F\) above 10; the residual weak-instrument bias runs like \(1/F\), worth about €{{nb11.f_leak}} per exposure here. A small complier share limits whom the estimate describes, not whether it is right.
Part 4 · When it breaks · the dangerous failure

A weak instrument is worse than no instrument

▶ LIVE what the estimator does as the first stage dies
Each point is the median IV estimate across many repeats at that first-stage \(F\); the band spans the 5th to 95th percentile. Drag \(\gamma\) down: the band explodes, and the centre drifts back toward the naive number.

The method divides by \(\pi\), and as the coin weakens the ratio fails in two ways:

  • The honest failure: the interval balloons; the data admit they know little.
  • The quiet failure: the centre creeps back toward the naive answer; dividing by a noisy near-zero resurrects exactly the bias the instrument was hired to remove.
  • No announcement: a weak instrument produces a plausible number with a plausible interval that is quietly wrong.
Report the first-stage \(F\), always
Below 10, stop: walk away, or use the interval on the next slide, which stays honest at any strength.
Part 4 · When it breaks · the repair

Build the interval by interrogating every candidate effect

▶ LIVE Anderson and Rubin: test each \(\beta_0\), keep the survivors
The green band is the set of effects the data cannot reject: the Anderson and Rubin confidence set, [{{nb11.ar_lo}}, {{nb11.ar_hi}}]. It never divides by \(\pi\), so a weak coin cannot corrupt it.
  • A question in a loop: if a candidate \(\beta_0\) were the truth, sales minus \(\beta_0 \times\) exposure should carry no trace of the coin:
\[ Y - \beta_0 X \;\perp\; Z \qquad \text{if } \beta_0 = \beta \] strip out the true effect, and what remains should look random with respect to a random coin
  • The interval: test that at 90% confidence for every \(\beta_0\) on the slider; the survivors are the interval.
  • Here, a good sign: the coin is strong, so AR [{{nb11.ar_lo}}, {{nb11.ar_hi}}] nearly matches the usual [{{nb11.iv_lo}}, {{nb11.iv_hi}}].
When it earns its keep
When \(F\) is small the two intervals part company, and AR is the one still telling the truth.
Part 4 · When it breaks · the fine print on the estimand

€{{nb11.iv_est}} is the effect on the people the coin could move

▶ LIVE the user base, split by how they respond to the coin
Push \(\gamma\): the complier slice (green) grows. The complier share is the first stage. A stronger coin speaks for more of the base.

Sort users by how the coin affects them:

  • Always-takers ({{nb11.st_share_always}}%): see the ad either way. The coin tells us nothing about them.
  • Never-takers ({{nb11.st_share_never}}%): never see it. Same silence.
  • Compliers ({{nb11.st_share_complier}}%): see the ad only because the coin favoured them. All of our information comes from these users.
  • The name: IV estimates the effect on compliers, the LATE (local average treatment effect); "local" means for this group, moved by this instrument.
  • The caveat: no defiers assumed (users who see the ad only when the coin does not favour them); a nudge that never repels is monotonicity.
Here, the fine print helps us
Compliers are exactly the users a higher bid would newly expose: the marginal customers the decision is about.

Part 5 · The decision · why a range is not enough

Same estimate, same interval, three different worlds

all three report "€{{nb11.iv_est}}, se €{{nb11.iv_se}}"
Three belief shapes with the same mean and the same standard error. A classical report cannot tell them apart, yet they disagree about how often a €18 bid pays.
  • Two numbers, no shape: a point and a spread do not pin down a distribution, and the decision questions live in the tail.
  • What a CI promises: over many repeated datasets, 90% of intervals built this way cover the truth; a statement about the procedure, not about this effect.
  • Not a flaw: you cannot integrate a procedure, so "the probability the exposure pays" is a question the tool was never built to answer.
The object that answers it
A posterior distribution over \(\beta\) given the data: once it exists, every pricing question becomes an integral over it.
Part 5 · The decision · the ten-minute primer

Prior times likelihood, renormalised

▶ LIVE update a belief about \(\beta\)
Widen the prior and the posterior collapses onto the data; tighten it onto a wrong value and you can drag the answer.

Here the unknown is \(\beta\), the euros one exposure causes; three objects, one rule:

\[ \underbrace{p(\beta \mid \text{data})}_{\text{posterior}} \;\propto\; \underbrace{p(\text{data} \mid \beta)}_{\text{likelihood}} \times \underbrace{p(\beta)}_{\text{prior}} \] what you believed, reweighted by how well each candidate \(\beta\) explains what you saw
  • Prior: belief before the data. Ours is deliberately vague.
  • Likelihood: the instrument's evidence, centred near €{{nb11.iv_est}}.
  • Posterior: the answer. A full distribution, not just a range.

Part 5 · The decision · the model, honestly

The default Bayesian IV has two flaws, and only the quiet one moves the money

  • The standard model: exposure and sales as one joint normal, tied by a correlation \(\rho\).
  • Its grade: €{{nb11.gauss_mean}}, off by €{{nb11.gauss_err}}, while plain 2SLS sits on the truth; two things about it are false:
\[ \begin{pmatrix} X \\ Y \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \begin{pmatrix} \alpha_0 + \gamma Z \\ \mu + \beta X \end{pmatrix},\; \Sigma(\rho) \right) \] the vivid flaw: the first row calls a yes/no event normal · the quiet flaw: the default prior on the error scales expects errors near €0.5, and sales errors run ≈ €13
The bias belongs to the quiet flaw
2SLS's correction \(\phi\) is a free coefficient, but this model must assemble it, \(\phi = \rho\,\sigma_Y/\sigma_X\): a prior squeezing \(\sigma_Y\) squeezes the bias correction itself.
  • Measured: the fit applies only {{nb11.gauss_s_pct}}% of the correction it owed.
  • Repair just that prior (standardise sales, or size it to the data): bias gone with the likelihood untouched, €{{nb11.scale_full_std_bias}} vs 2SLS, in lockstep with 2SLS on {{nb11.scale_full_std_agree}}/20 fresh markets; the next slide shows the whole curve.

The vivid flaw's own repair: a probit first stage that knows exposure is binary; shipped posterior €{{nb11.bayes_mean}} against a planted €{{nb11.true}}, on top of 2SLS.

with pm.Model() as probit_iv:
    # first stage: a BINARY treatment needs a binary link
    a0  = pm.Normal("a0", 0, 2)
    gam = pm.Normal("gam", 0, 2)
    eta = a0 + gam * Z                      # latent exposure index
    pm.Bernoulli("X", p=pm.math.invprobit(eta), observed=X)

    # the self-selection, named and estimated
    rho  = pm.Uniform("rho", -1, 1)
    beta = pm.Normal("beta", 0, 20)     # the euros we are buying
    sig  = pm.HalfNormal("sig", 10)

    # sales, shifted by the exposure slice that intent explains
    mu_y = mu0 + beta * X + rho * sig * resid(eta, X)
    pm.Normal("Y", mu_y, sig * pm.math.sqrt(1 - rho**2), observed=Y)
The one genuinely new number: \(\rho\)
2SLS works around the self-selection; this model measures it: \(\rho =\) {{nb11.rho}}, 90% credible [{{nb11.rho_lo}}, {{nb11.rho_hi}}], \(P(\rho > 0) = {{nb11.rho_p_pos}}\), the targeting alleged in Part 1 as a measured quantity.

Part 5 · The decision · the payoff

The question a posterior can answer and an interval cannot

▶ LIVE the posterior, cut at the price
Green mass is where the exposure pays. The bid cap is the price at which that mass falls to 90%: the posterior's 10th percentile, €{{nb11.cap}}.
\[ \Pr(\beta > c \mid \text{data}) \;=\; \int_{c}^{\infty} p(\beta \mid \text{data})\, d\beta \] the share of the posterior above the price: a probability a manager can act on
  • At €{{nb11.cost}}: the exposure pays with probability 1.00; every defensible \(\beta\) clears the price.
  • The bid cap, €{{nb11.cap}}: the highest price defensible at 90% confidence, the posterior's 10th percentile, not the estimate.
  • The coin flip, €{{nb11.coinflip}}: even odds at this price; never bid here.
  • Headroom: €{{nb11.headroom}} between the cap and today's €{{nb11.cost}} rate.
Why the cap is not the estimate
Bidding the point estimate (€{{nb11.iv_est}}) means being wrong half the time by construction; the cap builds the margin for error into the price itself.
Part 5 · The decision

Should we run a bigger lottery?

✋ Poll
At the €{{nb11.cost}} price the exposure pays with probability 1.00 and the headroom is €{{nb11.headroom}}. The analytics team offers to run a larger lottery for a few weeks to sharpen \(\beta\). What is that extra evidence worth?
C. Evidence is only worth what it can change. At €{{nb11.cost}}, every value of \(\beta\) the posterior still entertains says "buy", so no amount of sharpening can flip the action: the information is worth €{{nb11.voi}}, whatever the study costs. Move the price to the coin flip (€{{nb11.coinflip}}), where the decision is genuinely balanced, and the same study becomes worth €{{nb11.voi_coin}} per exposure. Buy evidence when the decision is close, not when the interval is wide.
Part 5 · The decision · pricing the study

Price the study before you run it

\[ \mathrm{VOI} \;=\; \mathbb{E}\big[\text{payoff with perfect information}\big] \;-\; \text{payoff of acting now} \] the value of information: what knowing \(\beta\) exactly would add, before committing
\[ \mathrm{VOI}(c) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\big[\max(\beta - c,\, 0)\big] \;-\; \max\big(\mathbb{E}[\beta] - c,\, 0\big) \] perfect information only helps where the sign of \(\beta - c\) is still in doubt
  • At the €{{nb11.cost}} price: VOI = €{{nb11.voi}}. The posterior never crosses the price, so knowledge buys nothing.
  • At the coin flip €{{nb11.coinflip}}: VOI = €{{nb11.voi_coin}}. Both actions are live, and information has a price.
The counter-intuitive rule
A wide interval is not a reason to study more; a close decision is.
  • Here: the interval is wide (€{{nb11.iv_lo}} to €{{nb11.iv_hi}}) and the decision is not close, so the right next spend is on exposures, not measurement.
  • Where it would bite: a rate near €{{nb11.cap}} erases the headroom, VOI climbs, and the study just declined becomes the best money on the table; re-price it whenever the rate card moves.

"Should we measure more?" is a comparison between the value of information and its cost, not a statistics question.

Eight things worth keeping

1 · Ask what assigned the treatment
When the thing you want to price chose itself, no amount of controls saves you. This question comes before any estimator.
2 · Find the coin
An instrument moves the treatment, is blind to intent, and is silent about the outcome. Lotteries, queues, rollouts, capacity limits, arbitrary rules.
3 · The estimator is a division
Reduced form over first stage: euros per flip over exposures per flip. If you cannot say your causal number as a ratio of two simple differences, you do not yet understand it.
4 · Report the F
A weak instrument is worse than none: precisely wrong, drifting back to the bias you fled. Below F = 10, walk away or use Anderson and Rubin.
5 · Precision is not validity
The naive interval was five times tighter and missed the truth. More rows shrink noise, never bias.
6 · Classical decides, Bayes prices
The interval settled the action. The posterior added P(pays), the bid cap, and the value of information. Two arms, one order.
7 · Buy evidence only when the decision is close
VOI was €{{nb11.voi}} at our price and €{{nb11.voi_coin}} at the coin flip. A wide interval is not, by itself, a reason to measure more.
8 · Know your regime
At n = 3,000 with a strong instrument the posterior must land on 2SLS (Bernstein and von Mises); Bayes buys the integral P(β > cost), not a different number. Its edge lives where data are thin, structure is hierarchical, or you genuinely know something first; and its defaults, like anyone's, need their units checked.
And the one that outranks them all
Your instrument's exclusion is untestable. It is defended by the design you chose, not by any output you can print. If the honest answer is "this coin might touch sales another way", the honest output is "I cannot measure this", and that is a result, not a failure.

Backup · tools this case did not need

Three tools for three named threats

Anderson and Rubin
Built live in Part 4, where it nearly matched 2SLS because \(F = {{nb11.f_stat}}\). Its value is at small \(F\), where it is the only interval that still means anything. Keep it in the drawer for the day the first stage is thin.
Abadie's κ
Compliers are invisible: you never learn who they are. Abadie's κ re-weights the sample so you can profile them, and check whether the LATE speaks for a population your bid can actually reach. Appendix 13.A.14.
Lee bounds
If the coin also changed who reports an outcome, the sample itself is selected and even IV is compromised. Lee bounds bracket the effect without assuming the problem away. Appendix 13.A.15.
Why they are backup and not the argument
Each answers a question this case does not raise (strong instrument, compliers already the bid's target, nothing attrits); wheeling them out anyway would be method theatre.
The transferable habit
Learn the named threats first (weak first stage, unknown compliers, selective reporting); the tools are easy to look up once you can name what went wrong.
